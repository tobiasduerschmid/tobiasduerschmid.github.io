# Backward Design (Understanding by Design)

Source: Wiggins, G. & McTighe, J. (2005), *Understanding by Design*
(expanded 2nd ed.). ASCD.

Backward Design is a framework for planning educational experiences —
courses, units, lessons, assignments — that starts from the end: what
should the learner understand or be able to do? Only once that's clear
do we decide how we'll know they've got it, and only then do we decide
what activities and content will get them there.

The framework is simple in structure but consequential in practice.
Most instructional design starts from content (what topics will I
cover?) or activities (what will we do?). Backward Design insists that
those are the *last* questions, not the first.

## The three stages

### Stage 1: Identify desired results

What should learners know, understand, and be able to do by the end?
Wiggins and McTighe distinguish three circles of priority:

**The innermost circle: enduring understandings.**
What big ideas should learners carry with them long after they forget
the details? What are the *transferable* insights, principles, or
understandings they should leave with?

An enduring understanding is not a fact ("the mitochondria is the
powerhouse of the cell"). It's a bigger idea ("cells have specialized
structures that enable different functions, and disruptions to those
structures cause specific diseases"). It's the kind of thing you want
them to know in ten years even if they don't remember the detailed
vocabulary.

**The middle circle: important to know and do.**
Core knowledge and skills that matter for this unit/course but aren't
necessarily life-long takeaways. Content they should master now to
build capability.

**The outer circle: worth being familiar with.**
Things it's helpful to have encountered, but that don't need deep
mastery.

The three-circle framework forces prioritization. Most curricula fail
this test — they treat everything as middle-circle, giving everything
equal weight and producing coverage at the expense of depth.

**Essential questions** are another Stage 1 tool. These are questions
that point at the enduring understandings — open-ended, provocative,
recurring through the unit. "What makes an argument compelling?"
"When should a scientist doubt their data?" "How does the same past
produce different histories?"

Essential questions are written for learners to genuinely engage with,
not just answer. They return across the unit, deepening with each
return. They resist single, tidy answers.

### Stage 2: Determine acceptable evidence

Before designing activities, decide how you'll know learners have
reached the desired results. What would count as evidence of the
enduring understandings? What would distinguish genuine understanding
from superficial performance?

Wiggins and McTighe distinguish:

**Performance tasks** — complex, authentic tasks where learners apply
their understanding to novel situations. These are the primary evidence
of understanding because understanding shows up most clearly in
transfer.

**Other evidence** — quizzes, tests, academic prompts, informal
checks, observations. These provide supporting evidence and coverage
of specific facts or skills.

This stage is where many course designs break. Instructors know the
content they want to cover but haven't specified what would count as
evidence of understanding. When they get to assessment, they default
to what's easy to assess (recall, recognition, basic application)
rather than what would actually demonstrate understanding (transfer,
explanation, self-monitoring).

### Stage 3: Plan learning experiences and instruction

*Now* decide what learners will do. With desired results and evidence
in hand, you can design activities that:

- Build toward the evidence directly — every major activity
  contributes to enabling a learner to demonstrate the desired
  results.
- Provide the practice, feedback, and scaffolding learners need to
  get there.
- Engage learners in the thinking that supports understanding, not
  just coverage.

The WHERETO acronym captures what good Stage 3 design attends to:

- **W**: Where is this headed? Why?
- **H**: Hook and hold attention.
- **E**: Equip learners with what they need.
- **R**: Rethink, revise, and reflect.
- **E**: Evaluate one's own understanding.
- **T**: Tailor to learners' needs and interests.
- **O**: Organize to maximize engagement and effectiveness.

## What the framework pushes against

### The twin sins of typical curriculum design

Wiggins and McTighe identify two common failure modes:

**The sin of coverage** — the curriculum marches through a list of
topics. The goal becomes "get through the textbook." Enduring
understanding is sacrificed to coverage. Students encounter many
topics but internalize few of them.

**The sin of activity-focused design** — the curriculum is planned
around engaging activities. "What can we do that's fun?" The
activities may be well-engineered, but they aren't clearly connected
to enduring understandings or evidence. Learners have an experience
but don't build the understanding the experience was supposedly
for.

Both sins share a feature: they *start from the wrong end*. Coverage
starts from content; activity-focused starts from what to do.
Backward Design starts from outcomes.

### The concept of understanding (six facets)

Wiggins and McTighe argue "understanding" is multifaceted. They
propose six facets of genuine understanding:

1. **Explanation** — can articulate the why and how, not just the
   what.
2. **Interpretation** — can make meaning, tell good stories, draw
   implications.
3. **Application** — can use knowledge effectively in new situations.
4. **Perspective** — can see from other points of view, recognize
   alternatives.
5. **Empathy** — can sense what others see or feel; find value in
   what might seem odd or alien.
6. **Self-knowledge** — knows the limits of their own understanding,
   where they might be wrong.

A learner who can recite a definition but can't explain why it
matters, apply it in a novel context, or notice when it fails has
surface knowledge, not understanding.

Assessments should tap multiple facets. A single multiple-choice test
accesses only a narrow slice of what understanding would look like.

## Applying backward design

### When designing a course from scratch

1. What are the enduring understandings? (3-6 for a semester-length
   course is typical.)
2. What are the essential questions? (Usually one or two per unit or
   major topic.)
3. What performance tasks would demonstrate those understandings?
4. What other evidence would confirm the supporting knowledge and
   skills?
5. What sequence of learning experiences builds learners toward
   those demonstrations?
6. What reading, practice, feedback, and scaffolding enable the
   sequence?

Only after these questions are answered does the instructor decide
what lectures, problem sets, labs, and projects will populate the
course.

### When evaluating an existing course/syllabus

Work backward through the three stages:

- Can you identify the enduring understandings? Or is it a list of
  topics?
- Are there essential questions that drive the units?
- What's the primary evidence of understanding? (Final exam? Projects?
  Authentic tasks?)
- Do the activities and content clearly serve the evidence?

Common findings:
- The course has an intended outcome but the assessment doesn't really
  test it.
- The course has clear assessments but the learning activities don't
  align.
- The course has activities that are good in themselves but don't
  build toward a clear outcome.
- The course is a list of topics with no visible endgame.

### When reviewing a single lesson or assignment

The same logic applies on smaller scales:

- What's the learning objective?
- What evidence would show the objective is met?
- Does this activity/assignment produce that evidence?

Often at this level, misalignment is obvious: the stated objective
says "apply concept X to novel situations," the assignment asks for
definition recall. The activity is well-executed but it doesn't match
the objective.

## The relationship to learning objectives

Backward Design is one theoretical home for the emphasis on learning
objectives. But note:

- Stage 1 outcomes are more than typical learning objectives. Learning
  objectives are often at the "important to know and do" level of the
  three circles. Enduring understandings are bigger than typical
  objectives.
- Good learning objectives support Stage 1 thinking but aren't a
  substitute for it. An objective like "understand the causes of
  World War I" isn't specific enough to drive Stage 2; "explain how
  nationalism, militarism, and alliance systems combined to make a
  localized conflict into a world war" is closer.

See `design/learning-objectives.md` for the objective-writing side.

## The transfer challenge

A deep commitment of Backward Design is **understanding enables
transfer**. Understanding isn't "knowing more facts"; it's developing
the kind of mental model that allows the learner to apply knowledge in
novel situations.

This has implications for assessment. Tests that use near-identical
contexts to what was taught don't distinguish surface knowledge from
understanding. Tests that require transfer — novel problems, novel
contexts, novel applications — do.

A good Backward Design course builds transfer-focused assessments
into Stage 2, then uses Stage 3 to prepare learners for them.

## Common design pitfalls

- **Learning objectives that aren't outcomes.** "Cover the causes of
  X" is a topic, not an outcome. "Explain the causes of X" is closer
  but still vague. "Argue for the most important cause of X using
  evidence from primary sources" is an outcome.
- **Assessments that don't match objectives.** Objective is about
  application; test is recall. Objective is about synthesis; test is
  routine application. This is extremely common.
- **Activities that entertain but don't build.** A class activity
  students enjoy is not automatically good design. What evidence does
  it produce? What understanding does it build?
- **Coverage anxiety.** The sense that you "have to cover" a list of
  topics often leads to shallow engagement with everything. Backward
  Design pushes toward depth and selection.
- **Starting from the textbook.** The textbook is a resource, not a
  curriculum. Letting it drive Stage 3 (activities and content)
  usually produces the coverage sin.

## Critique checklist

When reviewing a course design, syllabus, or curriculum:

1. Are there enduring understandings — big ideas learners should
   carry forward?
2. Are there essential questions that drive inquiry across the
   course/unit?
3. What counts as evidence of understanding? Are there authentic
   performance tasks, not just tests?
4. Do the assessments align with the stated outcomes?
5. Are learning activities designed to build toward the evidence?
6. Is there clear prioritization (the three circles), or is everything
   treated as equally important?
7. Does the course attend to multiple facets of understanding, or
   only the easy-to-assess ones?
8. Is transfer explicitly part of the design?

## Summary

**Backward Design = outcomes first, evidence second, activities third.**

Most design works in reverse order — starting from activities or
content, then retrofitting outcomes. Backward Design forces the
discipline of clarifying what success looks like before deciding how
to get there.

**Enduring understandings**: the big ideas that transfer.
**Essential questions**: the inquiry drivers.
**Evidence**: authentic demonstration of understanding.
**Activities**: in service of the evidence, not free-standing.

When critiquing existing materials, working backward through the
three stages usually reveals the root cause of whatever feels off —
usually alignment issues, prioritization issues, or the twin sins of
coverage and activity-focused design.
