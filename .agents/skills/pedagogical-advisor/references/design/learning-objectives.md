# Writing Learning Objectives

Learning objectives are the load-bearing infrastructure of educational
design. Almost every pedagogical judgment — alignment, assessment
quality, activity selection, difficulty calibration — depends on them.
Yet a remarkable amount of educational content has weak objectives, or
no objectives at all.

This file covers how to write objectives that are actually useful
rather than decorative, and how to recognize and fix the most common
problems.

## What a learning objective is for

A good learning objective does three things:

1. **Tells the learner what they should be able to do** by the end of
   the instruction. Not "what topics will be covered" (that's an
   agenda) or "what they'll experience" (that's a plan) — what they'll
   *be able to do*.
2. **Tells the instructor what to design toward**. Both activities and
   assessments should build toward this capacity.
3. **Tells the learner how to tell** whether they've got it.

Objectives that don't serve these three functions are often just
curriculum labels dressed up as objectives.

## The SMART framework (applied to objectives)

Objectives are typically called good when they're SMART:

- **S**pecific: clear and concrete, not vague.
- **M**easurable: can be assessed in some way.
- **A**chievable: realistic given context and learner.
- **R**elevant: serves a larger goal (course, program).
- **T**ime-bound: connected to a specific time frame (by the end of the
  unit, course, etc.).

**Specific and Measurable** are the ones most often missing. Here's
the test: if you can't imagine what evidence would tell you whether
the learner has met the objective, the objective is too vague.

## The ABCD structure (for writing clearly)

A widely-used rubric for writing objectives clearly:

- **A**udience: who is the learner (often implicit — "students will…")
- **B**ehavior: what observable action the learner will perform (this
  is where Bloom's action verbs belong)
- **C**ondition: under what circumstances the learner will perform
  (what resources, context, constraints)
- **D**egree: to what standard (what counts as acceptable performance)

Example:

> Students [audience] will be able to write a one-page analysis
> [behavior] of a primary source document they've not seen before
> [condition] identifying the author's argument, evidence, and two
> significant assumptions [degree].

Not every element is needed for every objective — sometimes condition
and degree are implied by context — but the four are useful for
checking that an objective is concrete enough.

## Action verbs by Bloom level

Learning objectives hinge on the verb. The verb specifies what
cognitive work the learner has to do. Using vague verbs (understand,
know, learn, appreciate) produces unassessable objectives.

A selection of reliable action verbs by Bloom level:

### Remember
- Define, describe, identify, label, list, match, name, outline,
  recall, recognize, select, state

### Understand
- Classify, compare, contrast, convert, describe (in own words),
  distinguish, estimate, explain, give examples, infer, interpret,
  paraphrase, predict, rephrase, summarize, translate

### Apply
- Apply, calculate, carry out, demonstrate, employ, execute,
  illustrate, implement, modify, operate, solve, use

### Analyze
- Analyze, attribute, break down, categorize, deconstruct, diagram,
  differentiate, distinguish, examine, infer, organize, outline,
  relate

### Evaluate
- Argue, assess, check, critique, decide, defend, evaluate, judge,
  justify, rank, rate, recommend, select, support, value

### Create
- Assemble, compose, construct, create, design, develop, devise,
  formulate, generate, hypothesize, invent, plan, produce, propose

### Verbs to avoid (too vague to assess)

- Understand, know, learn, appreciate, grasp, be familiar with,
  be aware of, internalize, gain insight into

These aren't bad words — they describe legitimate instructional
goals. But they aren't measurable as stated. "Students will
understand X" needs to become "students will be able to [specific
action] about X." The action verb is what makes it operational.

## Levels of objective granularity

A course typically has objectives at three levels:

**Course-level objectives** (3-6 typical)
Broad, often at the higher Bloom levels, connected to what the course
is *for*. "Students will design an experimental study addressing an
open question in cognitive psychology, defending their methodological
choices."

**Unit- or module-level objectives** (3-5 per unit)
More specific, feeding into the course-level ones. "Students will
analyze the validity threats in published studies and propose specific
improvements."

**Lesson- or session-level objectives** (1-4 per session)
Concrete and immediate. "Students will distinguish between internal
and external validity and identify the primary threats to each from a
given study description."

These levels should nest. Session objectives should ladder up to unit
objectives, which ladder up to course objectives. If they don't, it's
a sign that the unit or course structure is incoherent.

## Common problems with learning objectives

### Topics disguised as objectives

"The causes of World War I" isn't an objective. It's a topic. Add an
action verb: "Students will identify three causes of World War I and
explain how they interacted."

### Vague verbs

"Students will understand the causes of World War I." *What does
understanding mean here?* The verb doesn't tell you. Replace with a
specific action.

### Instructional activities as objectives

"Students will read *The Jungle* and discuss it in class." That's an
activity, not an outcome. The objective is what students will *be able
to do* as a result of that activity.

### Unassessable objectives

"Students will appreciate the elegance of mathematical proof."
Maybe they will, maybe they won't — you can't tell. Replace with
something you could assess: "Students will construct proofs that
meet criteria X, Y, Z" or "Students will identify which of two proofs
is more elegant and explain their reasoning."

### Mismatch between stated and real objective

Perhaps the most damaging pattern. The syllabus says "Students will
analyze primary sources"; the assessments are multiple-choice recall
questions. The real objective is recall; the stated objective was
analysis. Students learn to ignore the stated objectives.

### Mismatched Bloom levels

The objective says "analyze" but the assessment only tests "remember."
Check that the Bloom level of the objective is actually being assessed
at that level.

### Too many objectives

A course with 47 objectives has really told you nothing — it's a list
of topics re-labeled. Prioritize. Backward Design's three-circle
framework (enduring, important, worth being familiar with) helps.

### Objectives that are really standards

Standards are what an external body says learners should know/do.
Objectives are what *you* are designing your particular
instruction to achieve. They're related but not identical. A course's
objectives should derive from standards but may be more specific or
prioritized differently.

## The relationship to backward design

Learning objectives are Stage 1 artifacts in Backward Design (see
`design/backward-design.md`). The biggest objectives are the *enduring
understandings*; smaller objectives cluster below them.

A common failure mode: writing lots of small objectives (at the
unit/session level) without being clear on the big ones (course-level,
enduring understandings). This produces coverage-style courses — lots
of small competencies without a coherent overarching goal.

## Alignment with assessment

Each major objective should have at least one assessment that tests
it at the right Bloom level. A simple test: for each objective, can
you point at the specific assessment item(s) that test it? If not,
either the objective isn't being assessed (problematic if it's
important) or it isn't actually an objective (it's content to cover).

Building a *test blueprint* — a table showing which objectives are
tested by which items, at what Bloom level, with what weight —
forces this alignment before an exam is written.

## Alignment with learning activities

Each major objective should have activities that develop the capacity
being claimed. If the objective is "analyze case studies," there
should be instruction in case analysis methods and practice with
feedback. If no activities support the objective, it's aspirational,
not operational.

## Objectives and motivation

Objectives do more than organize; they can support motivation (via
expectancy — "here's what success looks like") or undermine it.

- Stated, achievable objectives support expectancy.
- Unstated or vague objectives leave learners uncertain about what
  they're supposed to learn.
- Objectives stated in a way that seems inaccessible (jargon, high
  register) can undermine belonging.

The rhetorical framing matters. "Students will be able to…" is
serviceable. Some programs have experimented with learner-voice
objectives — "By the end, I will be able to…" — which can feel more
inclusive.

## Writing objectives for different contexts

### For a standalone lesson or short session

Focus on session-level objectives only. 1-4 is a reasonable range.
Too many in a single session = overload.

### For a unit or module

3-5 unit objectives, each with a clear connection to 1-2 course-level
objectives. Session objectives derive from these.

### For a whole course

3-6 course-level objectives, ideally including some at higher Bloom
levels (analyze, evaluate, create) to avoid coverage-only design.

### For a program (multi-course)

Program-level objectives (learning outcomes) are typically at a
higher level and longer time scale. Individual courses contribute to
them. Course objectives should trace up to program ones.

## Critique checklist

When evaluating learning objectives in any artifact:

- Are there stated objectives? If so, are they at a useful level of
  granularity?
- Are they using action verbs at an appropriate Bloom level?
- Are they specific enough to be assessable?
- Is there alignment between the Bloom level of the objectives and
  the Bloom level of the assessments?
- Is the scope reasonable — not 40 objectives for one course?
- Is there a clear hierarchy (course → unit → session)?
- Does the instruction build toward the objectives?

If no objectives are stated, flag this as a high-priority issue.
Almost everything else in the design depends on them.

## A note on "I can" statements

Some educational contexts prefer "I can" statements — learner-voice
reframings of objectives. "I can identify the three causes of X and
explain how they interacted."

These are often used in K-12. The rhetorical move is valuable — it
puts the learner in the frame of capability. The core content
(specific action, appropriate Bloom level, measurable) still matters.

## Summary

**A useful learning objective tells the learner what they'll be able
to do, tells the instructor what to build toward, and tells everyone
how to tell whether it's been met.**

Get the verb right (action, Bloom-level appropriate). Make it specific
enough to assess. Keep the list short enough to be meaningful. Check
that assessments and activities actually align. Nest the levels —
session to unit to course to program.

**When an objective is missing or vague**, it's almost always the
biggest issue in the design, because everything else depends on it.
