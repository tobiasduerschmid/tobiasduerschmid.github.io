# Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

Source: CAST (2018), *Universal Design for Learning Guidelines
version 2.2*. http://udlguidelines.cast.org. Rose & Meyer (2002),
*Teaching Every Student in the Digital Age*. ASCD.

UDL is a framework for designing instruction that works for diverse
learners from the start, rather than designing for a "typical"
learner and retrofitting for others. The central insight: learner
variability is the norm, not the exception. Materials designed with
variability in mind from the beginning serve everyone better.

## The core idea

UDL distinguishes between **access** and **design**. Accommodations
give individual learners access to instruction designed for someone
else. Universal design builds variability into the instruction itself.

Accommodations are necessary and important — not everyone's needs can
be anticipated — but accommodation-heavy design is expensive,
reactive, and often leaves gaps. UDL argues that many accommodations
can be built into the default design, benefiting far more learners
than those formally identified as needing them.

Example: captions on educational video. A designed-without-captions
video requires captions to be added for deaf/HOH learners as an
accommodation. A UDL-designed video includes captions by default.
Captions benefit not just deaf/HOH learners but also:
- ESL learners
- Learners with reading preferences that benefit from dual-channel
- Learners in noisy environments
- Learners who want to skim
- Learners processing technical terminology

What was an accommodation for some becomes a default benefit for many.

## The three core principles

UDL articulates three principles, each with multiple guidelines.

### Principle 1: Multiple means of engagement (the *why* of learning)

Learners differ in what motivates them, what they find interesting,
and what supports their persistence. Provide multiple routes to
engagement.

**Guidelines**:
- **Recruiting interest**: offer choices, make tasks relevant, minimize
  threats and distractions.
- **Sustaining effort and persistence**: heighten salience of goals,
  vary demands and resources to support challenge, foster collaboration,
  increase mastery-oriented feedback.
- **Self-regulation**: promote learner beliefs that optimize motivation,
  facilitate personal coping skills, develop self-assessment and
  reflection.

### Principle 2: Multiple means of representation (the *what* of learning)

Learners perceive and comprehend information differently. Provide
multiple ways for information to be presented.

**Guidelines**:
- **Perception**: offer alternatives for auditory information (captions,
  transcripts), visual information (alt text, descriptions), and ways
  to customize display.
- **Language and symbols**: clarify vocabulary, support decoding,
  support cross-language understanding, illustrate through multiple
  media.
- **Comprehension**: activate or supply background knowledge,
  highlight patterns and relationships, guide information processing
  and visualization, maximize transfer and generalization.

### Principle 3: Multiple means of action and expression (the *how* of learning)

Learners differ in how they navigate and express what they know.
Provide multiple ways to act and to demonstrate learning.

**Guidelines**:
- **Physical action**: vary methods for response, optimize access to
  tools and assistive technologies.
- **Expression and communication**: use multiple media, build fluencies
  with graduated levels of support, use multiple tools for construction
  and composition.
- **Executive functions**: guide appropriate goal-setting, support
  planning and strategy development, facilitate managing information
  and resources, enhance capacity for monitoring progress.

## What UDL looks like in practice

UDL isn't a checklist but a design stance. Some concrete moves:

### In the format of materials

- **Multiple formats** where possible. Video lessons with transcripts
  AND captions. Readings available in text (screen-reader-friendly) as
  well as PDF. Key diagrams with clear alt text.
- **Chunkable content**: allowing learners to navigate at their own
  pace, with clear signaling of structure.
- **Accessible color and contrast**: not relying on color alone to
  convey meaning; sufficient contrast for visual clarity.
- **Clear visual hierarchy**: headings, consistent formatting, white
  space.

### In the examples and references

- **Examples drawn from varied contexts** — not only ones that assume
  particular cultural, geographic, or socioeconomic backgrounds.
- **Varied voices in references** — not only a narrow range of authors,
  disciplines, or traditions.
- **Representative imagery** — if depicting people, do so in ways that
  reflect diversity.

### In the assignments and assessments

- **Multiple ways to demonstrate understanding** where appropriate. A
  concept check could be answered in writing, orally, as a diagram, or
  as a worked problem. Not every assessment need standardize the
  expression mode.
- **Clear expectations** through rubrics and examples.
- **Scaffolded options** for learners who need more support (without
  locking them into a simpler path).
- **Reasonable time allocations** — not requiring unnaturally fast
  processing.

### In the engagement structure

- **Meaningful choices** — topics within a theme, problems within a
  set, formats within options. Supports autonomy (SDT) and engagement.
- **Relevance made explicit** — why does this matter? How does it
  connect to what learners care about?
- **Mastery-oriented feedback** — supporting a growth orientation.

## The "learning styles" myth — UDL vs. matching

**UDL is NOT about matching instruction to individual "learning
styles".** This is an important distinction. The "learning styles"
theory — that learners have specific modalities (visual, auditory,
kinesthetic) that they learn best through — has been repeatedly
shown to be unsupported by evidence. Matching instruction to
supposed styles produces no learning gains.

UDL's claim is different:

- "Learning styles": each learner has a preferred modality; instruction
  should be tailored to that preference.
- UDL: all learners benefit from instruction that offers multiple
  ways to engage, represent content, and demonstrate learning,
  because each learner faces different challenges with different
  content at different times.

The difference: UDL doesn't assume each learner has a fixed profile.
It assumes variability within and across learners, and that rich
materials support everyone better than narrow ones.

If someone brings up "learning styles" as a design principle, the
evidence-based response is to redirect: learning styles matching
doesn't work, but UDL-style multiplicity does.

## Common misapplications

### "UDL = optional accessibility"

UDL is often reduced to "make things accessible for students with
disabilities." This misses the design-for-everyone point. Good UDL
makes materials better for the whole class, not just flagged
learners.

### "UDL = no structure"

Some implementations of UDL have interpreted it as "let students do
whatever they want in whatever format they want." That's not what
it says. Structure is still needed. Goals are still needed.
Rigorous standards are still needed. UDL provides *multiple paths*
to those standards, not an absence of them.

### "UDL = more options = better"

Adding many options can be overwhelming and creates decision
fatigue. UDL's value is in the right options, not all options.
Meaningful choice over superficial choice.

### "UDL = watering things down"

UDL is about access to rigorous learning, not about making things
easier. The standards remain; the paths to them vary. Done well, UDL
supports higher-quality learning for everyone.

## Applying UDL to critique

When reviewing educational materials through a UDL lens:

### Engagement (why)
- Is relevance surfaced, or assumed?
- Are there meaningful choices for learners?
- Is the challenge level calibrated, with support available?
- Is the framing mastery-oriented or performance-oriented?
- Does the material support self-regulation?

### Representation (what)
- Are there multiple formats (visual, auditory, text)?
- Is content accessible — alt text, captions, contrast?
- Are technical terms and symbols supported?
- Is background knowledge activated/supplied?
- Are patterns and relationships made visible?

### Action and expression (how)
- Are there multiple ways to respond, interact, demonstrate?
- Are tools and technologies accessible?
- Is there support for planning, monitoring, self-assessment?
- Are expectations clear enough to navigate?

## Common red flags

- Video without captions or transcripts.
- Essential diagrams without descriptions or alt text.
- Color used to convey meaning (red means wrong; green means right —
  problematic for colorblind learners and in grayscale).
- Single format for expression (only written, only oral, only
  timed-test).
- Examples that systematically privilege particular backgrounds.
- Technical vocabulary without definition or scaffolding.
- Complex tasks without clear rubrics or expectations.
- One-size-fits-all timing that doesn't accommodate reasonable
  variation.
- Tight integration with tools that may not be accessible (specific
  software, specific platforms).

## UDL and equity

UDL has significant intersection with equity-focused design. Students
from under-represented backgrounds, first-generation students,
multilingual learners, and students with disabilities all benefit
disproportionately from UDL design — partly because the default
design of much education privileges specific backgrounds.

Designing with UDL principles reduces the burden on learners to
adapt to materials that weren't designed with them in mind.

## The legal dimension

In the US, the ADA and Section 504 require that educational materials
be accessible to learners with disabilities. In practice this often
means accommodations — retrofitting existing materials when a student
requests access.

UDL is pragmatic: proactively designed materials reduce the number of
accommodations needed, reduce the burden on individual learners to
self-advocate, and reduce the liability for the institution. Legal
compliance is a floor, not a ceiling.

## UDL in digital contexts

UDL has particular relevance for digital and online learning, where
the design decisions are often invisible to the designer but highly
consequential for the learner.

- Screen reader compatibility — semantic HTML, proper headings, alt
  text, meaningful link text.
- Keyboard navigability — forms, controls, interactive elements.
- Content that degrades gracefully — videos that work with captions
  only, diagrams that work with alt text only.
- Flexibility in pacing — avoid forced transitions, allow revisiting.
- Bandwidth-considerate — not all learners have high-speed internet.

Many of these are basic web accessibility standards (WCAG). UDL
extends beyond them into broader pedagogical design.

## Summary

**Design for variability from the start.** Don't build materials for
an imagined "typical" learner and accommodate exceptions; build for
the range of learners you actually have.

**Three principles** — multiple means of engagement (why), multiple
means of representation (what), multiple means of action/expression
(how).

**UDL is not "learning styles" matching** — it's providing richness
and flexibility to serve variability, not matching fixed profiles.

**Done well, UDL raises the floor and the ceiling** — more learners
succeed, and higher-quality learning is available to everyone.
