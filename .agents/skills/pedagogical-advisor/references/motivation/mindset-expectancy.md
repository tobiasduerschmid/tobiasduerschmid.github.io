# Mindset, Expectancy, and Value

Sources: Dweck (2006), *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success*. Random
House. Wigfield & Eccles (2000, 2020), expectancy-value theory. Yeager &
Dweck (2020), "What Can Be Learned from Growth Mindset Controversies?"
*American Psychologist*. The *Handbook of Competence and Motivation*
(Elliot, Dweck, & Yeager, eds., Guilford 2017).

Two closely related motivation theories that complement Self-Determination
Theory. Where SDT focuses on psychological needs, mindset and
expectancy-value focus on learner *beliefs* about ability and the value
of tasks. These beliefs shape effort, persistence, and achievement.

## Mindset theory (Dweck)

### Fixed vs. growth mindset

A **fixed mindset** is the belief that ability is largely innate and
unchangeable — you're either a "math person" or you're not; you either
have "the gift" or you don't.

A **growth mindset** is the belief that ability can be developed through
effort, strategy, and feedback. Ability isn't fixed; it's grown.

The consequences of these beliefs compound:

- Fixed-mindset learners interpret struggle as evidence they don't have
  the ability. They avoid challenges (why risk confirming low ability?),
  give up when things are hard, see effort as a sign of lack of ability,
  and ignore or reject feedback that contradicts their self-concept.
- Growth-mindset learners interpret struggle as evidence they're in the
  zone where growth happens. They seek challenge, persist through
  difficulty, see effort as essential, and use feedback as information.

Neither mindset is about being right about the true origins of ability.
Both are beliefs — and the belief itself affects outcomes independent of
the underlying ability.

### What the research shows

- Mindset interventions can improve academic outcomes, *especially for
  lower-achieving students and in structurally challenging contexts*
  (recent large-scale replications — Yeager et al. 2019 National Study of
  Learning Mindsets — show small but meaningful effects in these groups).
- The effects are not huge in general populations — the early claims were
  oversold. But targeted mindset interventions remain one of the
  cheaper interventions with measurable academic effects.
- Mindset interacts with context. A growth-mindset message from an
  instructor whose other signals are fixed (curved grading, "only the
  top students can…" rhetoric) doesn't land. Mindset has to be
  supported structurally, not just rhetorically.
- Praising *effort or strategy* rather than *intelligence or ability*
  supports growth mindset. "You worked hard on that" or "Great
  approach" beats "You're so smart" or "You're a natural."

### Design implications

**Framing the material and course**:
- Name that the material is hard, and that struggle is normal. Don't
  pretend anything is trivial.
- Frame success as the result of effort + strategy + feedback, not
  talent.
- Avoid language that implies talent is doing most of the work.
- Include real examples of learners who initially struggled and then
  mastered the material.

**Feedback and praise**:
- Praise process (effort, strategy, improvement) rather than person
  (ability, intelligence, smarts).
- When a learner fails, frame it as information about what to try next,
  not evidence about them.
- Avoid "you're so smart" — it anchors to a fixed-ability frame.
- Say "not yet" instead of "wrong" where appropriate — it signals
  growth is ongoing.

**Course structure**:
- Provide opportunities for revision, retakes, mastery learning. This
  structurally says "the goal is getting there," not "the goal is
  ranking."
- Avoid curved grading where possible — it signals "only some can
  succeed," a fixed-mindset structure.
- Normalize productive struggle — build it into the explicit
  curriculum.

### Critique checklist

Look for:
- Language signaling fixed ability ("this is for advanced students only,"
  "if you're not a natural at this…")
- Praise or feedback that anchors to ability/intelligence rather than
  process
- Grading or assessment structures that create scarcity of success
- No mechanism for mastery or revision
- Implicit messaging about who succeeds in this domain

### Common misapplications of growth mindset

- **Pretending everything is achievable.** Growth mindset isn't denying
  that some things are hard or require specific prerequisites. It's
  affirming that ability can grow through work.
- **"Effort is enough."** Effort without good strategy doesn't produce
  growth. Growth mindset should be paired with teaching effective
  strategies.
- **Lip service.** Posting "growth mindset" posters doesn't change
  outcomes. The climate, grading, feedback, and framing all have to
  cohere.

## Expectancy-value theory (Eccles & Wigfield)

### The core claim

A learner's motivation to engage in a task is predicted by two factors:

- **Expectancy of success**: how much do I believe I can succeed if I
  put in effort?
- **Value of the task**: how much do I care about succeeding at this?

Motivation is roughly the product. If either is near zero, motivation
is near zero. High expectancy with no value means "I could do it but
why bother." High value with no expectancy means "I care but I'll
never get there." Both have to be reasonably high for sustained
engagement.

### Sub-components of value

Value has four components worth distinguishing:

1. **Attainment value** — does succeeding at this task matter to my
   sense of self? Is this consistent with who I am or want to be?
2. **Intrinsic value** — is the activity itself enjoyable or
   interesting?
3. **Utility value** — does succeeding here help me achieve other
   goals I have?
4. **Cost** — what do I give up (time, effort, foregone alternatives,
   psychological costs) by engaging? High cost erodes effective value.

Different students bring different mixes. Some are driven mostly by
intrinsic interest; some by utility for career goals; some by
identity-based attainment.

### What the research shows

- **Utility-value interventions** — brief interventions where students
  reflect on how course content connects to their lives and goals —
  produce measurable improvements in outcomes, especially for
  underrepresented and lower-performing students.
- **Expectancy** is malleable. Building it through early successes,
  role models, and explicit "you can do this if you do X" messaging has
  real effects.
- **Cost** is often underweighted in design. Assignments that have
  heavy time cost, high social cost, or high opportunity cost produce
  less motivation than their objective content would predict.

### Design implications

**Supporting expectancy**:
- Calibrate difficulty so that success is achievable with effort.
- Scaffold so that progress is visible and feasible.
- Use worked examples and scaffolded practice before independent
  challenge.
- Surface role models and examples of learners like the target
  audience succeeding.
- Provide clear rubrics and expectations so learners know what success
  looks like.

**Supporting value**:
- Make utility-value explicit. Connect content to what learners are
  doing, will do, care about.
- Support identity connection. Who does this? Why does it matter in
  their field/life?
- Use examples drawn from contexts meaningful to the audience.
- Include meaningful choice (also SDT's autonomy — the theories
  interact).

**Reducing cost**:
- Be realistic about workload. Chronically overlong assignments
  reduce motivation.
- Reduce non-essential cognitive costs (opaque instructions, hard-to-
  access materials).
- Be aware of social costs — public failure in front of peers, being
  asked to reveal vulnerabilities.

### Utility-value intervention specifics

The best-established expectancy-value intervention is simple and
worth knowing. Students are asked, a few times across a course, to
write briefly about how something from recent course content connects
to their own lives, goals, or interests. That's it.

- **Produces measurable gains** in grades, especially for lower-
  performing students.
- **Cheap** — a 10-15 minute writing activity, done a few times per
  term.
- **Generalizable** — works across subjects.
- **Caveat**: the intervention works partly because it's genuine.
  Mechanical "list three uses" prompts don't have the same effect.
  The writing has to be personal and reflective.

### Critique checklist

Look for:
- Is the *why* of the material made visible in the content?
- Are connections to learner goals or interests surfaced?
- Are examples drawn from contexts that matter to the audience?
- Is the difficulty calibrated to support expectancy?
- Is the workload/cost calibrated?

## Combining mindset, expectancy-value, and SDT

These three theories overlap substantially and together provide a
useful diagnostic for motivation problems.

- **Low engagement despite effort from instructor**: check expectancy
  (too hard? scaffolding missing?) and cost (too much work?).
- **Disengagement early**: check value (why should they care?).
- **Avoidance of challenge**: check mindset (fixed framing?) and
  competence support.
- **Compliance without investment**: check autonomy (is the structure
  controlling?) and identified regulation.
- **Performance anxiety**: check climate (threat-laden?), mindset
  (fixed?), and competence support (achievable success?).

When critiquing an instructional artifact for motivation:

1. Does it build expectancy through appropriate scaffolding and clear
   success criteria?
2. Does it make value visible through utility, interest, or identity
   connections?
3. Does it frame ability as malleable through effort and strategy?
4. Does it support the three basic psychological needs (autonomy,
   competence, relatedness)?

Most motivational deficits in educational content can be traced to one
or more of these.

## Some things to avoid

- **Fake praise.** Learners, especially older ones, detect inflated
  praise and it undermines credibility of subsequent feedback.
- **Empty "relevance" assertions.** Telling learners "this is useful
  for your career" without showing *how* doesn't build value.
- **Mindset posters without mindset structures.** Language saying
  "anyone can succeed with effort" combined with curved grading and
  talent-framed praise is incoherent and learners see through it.
- **Over-reliance on extrinsic motivation.** Grades, points, and
  rewards produce short-term compliance but tend to undermine
  long-term intrinsic and identified motivation. Use sparingly.

## Summary

- **Growth mindset**: ability is malleable through effort and strategy.
  Build this belief through framing, feedback, and structure.
- **Expectancy**: learners engage when they believe success is
  achievable with effort. Support through scaffolding, clear
  expectations, and visible progress.
- **Value**: learners engage when success matters to them. Support
  through utility-value, identity connection, and intrinsic interest.
- **Cost**: keep the cost (time, effort, emotional) reasonable.

Together with SDT, these give you a nearly complete motivation
framework for educational design and critique.
