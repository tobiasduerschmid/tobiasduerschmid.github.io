# Self-Determination Theory

Source: Ryan & Deci (2000, 2017), *Self-Determination Theory: Basic
Psychological Needs in Motivation, Development, and Wellness*. Also the
SDT chapters in the *Handbook of Competence and Motivation* (Elliot,
Dweck, & Yeager, eds., Guilford 2017).

SDT is the most influential and best-empirically-supported theory of
motivation for education. Its central claim is that humans have three
basic psychological needs, and the quality of motivation — not just the
amount — depends on whether those needs are being met.

When instructional material or a course environment fails to motivate
learners, SDT typically predicts what's going wrong and what to do about
it.

## The three basic psychological needs

**Autonomy**: the need to feel that one's actions are self-endorsed — that
you're doing what you're doing because it aligns with your values and
interests, not because you're being coerced.

**Competence**: the need to feel effective in interactions with the
environment — to experience mastery and growth.

**Relatedness**: the need to feel connected to others, to care and be cared
for, to have a sense of belonging in a social group.

When these three needs are supported, motivation is higher quality
(intrinsic and well-internalized extrinsic motivation dominate) and
outcomes are better — academically, emotionally, and in terms of
persistence. When they're thwarted, motivation erodes or becomes
controlled and fragile.

## The spectrum of motivation quality

SDT's second major contribution is the idea that motivation isn't one
thing. There's a continuum from amotivation (no motivation at all)
through several forms of extrinsic motivation to intrinsic motivation:

1. **Amotivation** — no motivation to engage; typically produces
   disengagement.

2. **External regulation** — doing something because of external reward
   or punishment. "I'll do the homework because if I don't I'll get a
   bad grade." This motivation is fragile: remove the reward/punishment
   and the behavior stops.

3. **Introjected regulation** — internalized pressure, but externally
   sourced. "I'll do the homework because I'd feel guilty if I didn't."
   The pressure is in the learner but still controlling.

4. **Identified regulation** — the learner has accepted the value of the
   activity. "I'll do the homework because I want to understand this
   material." More durable and healthy.

5. **Integrated regulation** — the value is consistent with other values
   the learner holds. "I do the homework because I identify as someone
   who pursues understanding." Most healthy form of extrinsic motivation.

6. **Intrinsic motivation** — doing the activity because it's inherently
   enjoyable or interesting. The learner is engaged for the activity's
   own sake.

The key educational insight: **you can't rely on intrinsic motivation for
most course content** — students won't find everything inherently
fascinating. But you *can* support the internalization of extrinsic
motivation, moving learners from external regulation toward identified
and integrated regulation. That transformation happens in climates that
support the three basic psychological needs.

## Design implications for autonomy support

Autonomy-supportive design gives learners meaningful choice and voice,
explains rationales for required activities, acknowledges learners'
perspectives, and minimizes coercive language and controlling structure.

**Concrete moves**:

- **Offer meaningful choices** where possible. Within an assignment:
  choose the topic, choose which of several problems to do, choose a
  format. Across a course: optional activities, multiple paths.
- **Provide rationales** for required activities. "You're doing this
  because it builds the skill you'll need for…" rather than "do this
  because I'm telling you to."
- **Acknowledge difficulty and frustration.** "This is legitimately
  hard; here's why" beats "this is easy, just do it."
- **Avoid controlling language.** "You should," "you must," "you have
  to" produce controlled motivation. "You can," "consider," "one option
  is" support autonomy without losing direction.
- **Use informational rather than evaluative feedback.** Feedback that
  describes what happened and what to adjust supports autonomy.
  Feedback that's pure evaluation (grades, rankings) tends toward
  controlling.

### Things that thwart autonomy

- Excessive surveillance (plagiarism-focused design, required
  participation tracking)
- Arbitrary rules without rationale
- Grade/ranking-heavy classroom climates
- Rigid procedures where judgment could be used
- Language that signals "because I said so"

### The mechanic's fallacy

Be careful with "gamification" as autonomy support. Extrinsic rewards
(points, badges, leaderboards) can *undermine* intrinsic motivation in
activities that were already intrinsically motivating — the so-called
undermining effect. Gamification that feels manipulative often reduces
motivation quality even if it increases short-term engagement.

The better autonomy-supportive move: make the intrinsic interest of the
activity visible rather than adding external rewards on top.

## Design implications for competence support

Competence support means designing so that learners experience
progress, success, and growth — without the false-success of trivial
tasks or the crushing failure of impossible ones.

**Concrete moves**:

- **Calibrate difficulty** so that success is achievable with effort.
  The zone where the learner has to work hard but can get there is where
  competence is built.
- **Make progress visible.** Milestones, cumulative skills built,
  before-and-after comparisons. Learners often underestimate their own
  growth.
- **Give informational feedback.** Feedback that tells the learner what
  they're doing well and what to work on, specifically. Feedback that
  supports self-monitoring builds competence; feedback that's purely
  evaluative often undermines it.
- **Build early successes.** Especially for anxious learners or those
  who don't yet see themselves in the field. Early wins before the
  hardest content.
- **Scaffold, don't rescue.** Provide enough support that the learner
  can succeed through their own effort. Too much help signals that
  you don't believe they can do it. Too little strands them.

### Things that thwart competence

- Impossible or near-impossible tasks without sufficient support.
- "Learning" that's actually guessing.
- Feedback that's too global ("good job") or too self-focused ("you're
  smart") to be useful.
- Comparative grading structures (curves) that make success contingent
  on peers' failure.
- Climates where errors are framed as indicators of ability rather than
  as learning opportunities.

## Design implications for relatedness support

Relatedness is often overlooked in content design but is central in
course and program design. Learners who feel they belong and are cared
about persist and learn better than those who don't.

**Concrete moves**:

- **Signal that the instructor/author cares about learners' success.**
  Not just content delivery, but investment in outcomes.
- **Use inclusive examples, references, and framing.** Materials where
  learners can see themselves are more relatable.
- **Build community where possible.** Cohort structures, group
  activities, spaces for learners to connect with each other.
- **Be warm in the language.** Not saccharine, but human. Acknowledge
  learners as people, not as input-output functions.
- **Respond to learners individually** when possible. Generic
  feedback feels impersonal; responsive feedback signals mattering.

### Things that thwart relatedness

- Cold, detached, or overly formal tone.
- Group work structured in ways that produce social exclusion or
  loafing rather than cooperation.
- Climates where certain identities feel unwelcome.
- No mechanism for learners to feel seen or responded to individually.

## The interaction of the three needs

The three needs interact. Autonomy and competence often reinforce each
other — learners with more choice engage more deeply and build more
competence. Relatedness enables the other two — learners who feel cared
for accept challenges (building competence) and engage with content
(expressing autonomy).

Conversely, threats to one need often threaten others. A climate of
surveillance threatens autonomy *and* relatedness. A curve-graded
course threatens competence (many students will fail) *and*
relatedness (peers are competitors). Being aware of these interactions
helps in diagnosing motivation problems.

## Applying SDT to critique

**In a syllabus or course design**:
- Is there any meaningful choice? (Autonomy)
- Is the difficulty curve going to support or erode competence for
  different learners?
- What signals does the tone/framing send about belonging? (Relatedness)
- How is grading structured? Does it encourage or erode competence?

**In a homework assignment**:
- Is there autonomy in approach, topic, or format?
- Is the challenge calibrated?
- Are the instructions and tone relationship-sustaining or
  bureaucratic?

**In a lecture or textbook chapter**:
- Does it signal rationale for why learners should care?
- Does it build competence through appropriate scaffolding?
- Does the language and example selection support relatedness for a
  diverse audience?

**When a student or class isn't engaging**:
- Which need is being thwarted? Diagnose before prescribing.
- Controlling structures thwart autonomy. Too-hard or too-easy tasks
  threaten competence. Impersonal climates undercut relatedness.

## SDT and grades

One of the most practical SDT findings is the tension between extrinsic
incentives (like grades) and intrinsic motivation. In general:

- Heavy grade-focus produces controlled motivation and can undermine
  intrinsic interest.
- De-emphasizing grades (while still giving honest feedback) supports
  autonomy and can preserve intrinsic motivation.
- Formative assessment that provides informational feedback without
  stakes is better for learning than frequent high-stakes testing.

Practical implications: where possible, use grades sparingly and
strategically. Low-stakes formative assessments should not feel like
mini-judgments. Revision opportunities, flexible policies, and
mastery-based grading all align with SDT.

## Common misapplications of SDT

- **Mistaking autonomy for permissiveness.** Autonomy support isn't
  letting students do whatever they want. It's providing rationale,
  meaningful choice within structure, and respect for their
  perspective. Structure is compatible with autonomy support.
- **Mistaking relatedness for friendship.** Instructors don't have to
  be friends with students. Relatedness means being seen, being cared
  for, being treated with respect.
- **Mistaking competence support for easy tasks.** Making things easy
  doesn't build competence. Making success reachable through genuine
  effort does.
- **Adding gamification without rationale.** Extrinsic rewards on top
  of intrinsically motivating activities can backfire. Use sparingly.

## Summary

SDT's contribution is a diagnosis framework for motivation problems
and a design framework for motivation-supportive environments. When
motivation is the issue — learners aren't engaging, dropping out,
doing the minimum — ask: is autonomy supported? Is competence
achievable through effort? Is relatedness present? The answer points
at the intervention.

And for the long game: the quality of motivation matters as much as
the quantity. A course that produces identified regulation — learners
who've internalized the value of the material — produces more durable
learning than one that uses grades to compel engagement.
