# Multimedia Learning (Mayer's Principles)

Source: Mayer, R. E. (2009), *Multimedia Learning* (2nd ed.), Cambridge
University Press; Mayer (2014), *The Cambridge Handbook of Multimedia
Learning*, Cambridge University Press; Mayer & Moreno (2003), "Nine Ways
to Reduce Cognitive Load in Multimedia Learning," *Educational Psychologist*.

Mayer's framework is the most practical guidance available for designing
instruction that combines words (spoken or written) and pictures (images,
animations, video). It rests on three cognitive assumptions and generates
twelve specific principles, each with dozens of controlled experiments
supporting it.

## The three foundational assumptions

1. **Dual-channel assumption.** People process auditory and visual
   information through partially separate channels in working memory.
   This means visual and auditory presentation can be complementary —
   using both channels expands effective capacity.
2. **Limited-capacity assumption.** Each channel has limited capacity.
   Overloading either produces the losses we see in cognitive-load-
   theory terms.
3. **Active-processing assumption.** Meaningful learning requires the
   learner to actively select relevant information, organize it into
   coherent representations, and integrate it with prior knowledge.
   Passive exposure to multimedia doesn't produce learning even when
   all the content is there.

Together these assumptions yield the 12 principles below. The principles
are organized into three groups by what they address.

## Group 1: Principles for reducing extraneous processing

These principles address extraneous cognitive load — load imposed by the
design that doesn't contribute to learning.

### 1. Coherence principle
**People learn better when extraneous words, pictures, and sounds are
*excluded* rather than included.**

Background music, "interesting but irrelevant" details, decorative images,
unrelated stories — these all draw processing resources away from the
learning material. The intuition is that they make content engaging;
the evidence is that they depress learning.

- *In slides*: remove decorative images that don't carry content.
  Remove background music in educational videos.
- *In videos*: cut the "hook" that has nothing to do with the content.
- *In textbooks*: cut the "interesting sidebars" that don't directly
  support the main content.

### 2. Signaling principle
**People learn better when cues that highlight the organization of
essential material are added.**

Learners don't automatically know what to attend to. Cues — headings,
emphasis (bold, color), verbal signaling ("notice that…", "the key
point is…"), visual callouts — direct attention to what matters.

- Use section headings that name the relationship ("Three causes of X")
  not just the topic ("X").
- In diagrams, highlight or arrow the elements currently being discussed.
- In narration, use signaling phrases ("first," "more importantly,"
  "the critical thing is…").
- But don't over-signal — if everything is highlighted, nothing is.

### 3. Redundancy principle
**People learn better from graphics and narration than from graphics,
narration, and on-screen text.**

When the same information is delivered by both narration and identical
on-screen text, working memory is divided between processing the text
and processing the narration, to no benefit. This is one of the most
counterintuitive Mayer principles and one of the most violated.

- *In slides*: don't put every word you're going to say on the slide.
  The slide should be a complement to what you're saying, not a
  transcript of it.
- *In video with narration*: avoid simultaneous on-screen captions of
  the narration (unless for accessibility, in which case ideally make
  them available on-demand).
- *Exception*: brief key terms or technical phrases can be on-screen
  when first introduced — especially if unfamiliar — but only briefly.
  Short technical labels are often fine; full prose is not.
- *Exception for accessibility*: captions for learners with hearing
  needs should always be available. The redundancy principle is
  about not *imposing* redundancy by default on all learners.

### 4. Spatial contiguity principle
**People learn better when corresponding words and pictures are placed
near each other.**

When the text describing a part of a diagram is physically adjacent to
that part (embedded labels), rather than in a separate legend, split-
attention is avoided. This is CLT's split-attention effect in multimedia
form.

- *In diagrams*: use labels integrated into the figure, not legends.
- *In slides*: place descriptive text near the element it describes.
- *In worked examples*: place the explanation of each step next to the
  step.
- *In layouts generally*: if learner has to scan to find the match,
  there's a spatial-contiguity cost.

### 5. Temporal contiguity principle
**People learn better when corresponding words and pictures are presented
simultaneously rather than successively.**

A diagram followed by narration describing it, or narration followed by
the diagram, each force the learner to hold material in working memory
while waiting for the complement. Simultaneous presentation avoids this.

- *In videos*: animate or reveal diagram elements at the moment the
  narration refers to them.
- *In tutorials*: don't explain a concept first and then show a
  diagram several paragraphs later. Integrate them.

## Group 2: Principles for managing essential processing

These principles address intrinsic cognitive load — how to help learners
process complex material that has irreducible essential complexity.

### 6. Segmenting principle
**People learn better when a multimedia message is presented in
learner-paced segments rather than as a continuous unit.**

A continuous 20-minute video doesn't let the learner pause to
consolidate. Breaking it into segments with natural stopping points,
each the learner can advance on their own, works better.

- *Educational video*: break into segments of ~2-6 minutes, each with
  a user-advance transition.
- *Online modules*: chunk content into screens the learner advances
  through rather than long scrolls.
- *Lectures*: punctuate with pauses, check-for-understanding moments,
  discussion, so the segmenting happens even in continuous delivery.

### 7. Pre-training principle
**People learn better from a multimedia message when they know the names
and characteristics of the main concepts.**

If a video is about how a part of an engine works, introducing the names
of the parts beforehand reduces working-memory load during the main
presentation. The learner doesn't have to simultaneously learn terminology
and the process.

- *In videos and lectures*: cover key terminology before the main
  content. A glossary page, a quick flashcard drill, a pre-viewing
  reading.
- *In textbook chapters*: introduce terminology with clear definitions
  before using it in complex explanations.
- *Especially important for novices*: experts already have the
  terminology, so pre-training may not help (or may slow them down —
  expertise reversal).

### 8. Modality principle
**People learn better from graphics and narration than from graphics
and on-screen text.**

Visual channel is busy processing the graphics; adding on-screen text
forces it to also process words. Using narration instead frees the
visual channel.

- *In educational videos and animations*: narrate rather than put text
  on-screen alongside the animation.
- *In e-learning modules*: narrate with visuals rather than presenting
  text with static images.
- *Exception*: when learners need to control pacing (can't replay
  narration easily), or when terminology is unfamiliar and they need
  to see it spelled, written text may be necessary.
- *Exception for accessibility*: again, captions for deaf/HOH learners.

## Group 3: Principles for fostering generative processing

These principles address germane cognitive load — activating the
learner's schema-construction work.

### 9. Multimedia principle
**People learn better from words and pictures than from words alone.**

This is the foundational principle. Combining visual and verbal
representations helps learners build integrated mental models that
neither alone provides.

- *In any content*: if an idea can be shown as well as told, do both
  (within the other principles' constraints).
- *Not*: every paragraph needs a picture. The pictures need to be
  relevant and informative (coherence principle).

### 10. Personalization principle
**People learn better from multimedia lessons when words are in
conversational rather than formal style.**

First-person and second-person language ("we'll look at", "notice how
you can…") produces better learning than third-person formal style
("the learner will observe", "one can see"). This seems to activate
more social-processing engagement.

- *In narration*: use "you" and "we" rather than "one" or passive voice.
- *In textbooks*: direct address and first-person reasoning work well.
- *Caveat*: don't cross into cringe. Overly casual or "relatable" can
  backfire if it reads as condescending or forced.

### 11. Voice principle
**People learn better when the narration is in a human voice rather
than a machine voice.**

A natural human voice elicits better learning than synthetic voice,
probably because listeners process it as social communication. With
AI voices improving this effect is shrinking, but some residual
effect remains.

- *In educational video*: use human narration when possible.
- *In e-learning*: the voice matters; budget for good narration.

### 12. Embodiment principle
**People learn better when an on-screen agent displays human-like
gestures and movements.**

Seeing an instructor who gestures (pointing at what's being discussed,
facial expressions, natural hand movements) improves learning over
a purely disembodied voice or a static speaker. Embodiment seems to
engage social-cognitive processing.

- *In videos*: consider showing the instructor in addition to / instead
  of pure screencast.
- *In animated agents*: agents that gesture outperform ones that don't.

(Later work has added principles for adaptive learning, generative
activities, and engagement, but the core 12 remain.)

## Some newer principles worth knowing

- **Image principle**: adding a static image of the instructor doesn't
  necessarily improve learning (the embodiment principle covers dynamic
  presence, not static image).
- **Generative learning principle**: asking learners to engage in
  generative activities during multimedia (summarizing, self-explaining,
  drawing, teaching) improves learning. This connects to ICAP.
- **Guided discovery principle**: pure discovery without guidance
  produces poor learning, but discovery *with guidance* can outperform
  direct instruction for motivation and depth.

## Applying Mayer to design and critique

When looking at any multimedia-heavy content (slides, videos, online
modules, interactive tutorials), the 12 principles give you a concrete
checklist.

**Quick audit for a slide or video**:
- Coherence: is anything here not serving the learning?
- Signaling: is what matters made visually and verbally salient?
- Redundancy: is there text on screen that duplicates what's being said?
- Spatial contiguity: are labels / explanations near what they describe?
- Temporal contiguity: do visuals and narration align in time?
- Segmenting: is the pacing learner-controlled or at least chunked?
- Pre-training: is terminology introduced before being used in depth?
- Modality: is the right channel being used for the right content?
- Multimedia: are words and pictures both being used, and integrated?
- Personalization: conversational voice? "you," "we"?
- Voice: human narrator?
- Embodiment: if there's an on-screen agent, do they behave naturally?

**Red flags common in real materials**:

1. Dense text slides read verbatim by narrator (violates redundancy,
   modality, multimedia)
2. Decorative stock photos with no learning relevance (violates coherence)
3. Diagrams with separate legends (violates spatial contiguity)
4. 60-minute videos with no natural breaks (violates segmenting)
5. Jargon dropped without prior definition (violates pre-training)
6. Formal "one" or passive voice in explainer content (violates
   personalization)
7. Synthesized-voice narration with no visible speaker (often
   violates voice and embodiment)

## The relationship to CLT

Mayer's principles are essentially CLT applied to multimedia:
- Coherence, signaling, redundancy, spatial and temporal contiguity →
  reducing extraneous load
- Segmenting, pre-training, modality → managing intrinsic load
- Multimedia, personalization, voice, embodiment → fostering generative
  (germane) processing

If the analysis task is "critique a slide deck" or "redesign a video
lesson," Mayer's principles are often the right vocabulary. If the
analysis is broader ("is this lecture well-structured?"), CLT and the
other files are more central and Mayer is supporting.

## Where Mayer's principles push back on common practice

A few places where following Mayer goes against common instructional
design conventions:

- **Text on slides during a spoken presentation**: common; Mayer says
  minimize it.
- **Elaborate intro sequences / "hooks"**: common; Mayer's coherence
  principle questions them.
- **"Engaging" decorative visuals**: common; Mayer says they're often
  extraneous load.
- **Background music**: common in e-learning; Mayer shows it reduces
  learning.

When critiquing an artifact that has these features, be confident about
the recommendation (the evidence is strong) but be pragmatic about the
constraints the creator was operating under.
