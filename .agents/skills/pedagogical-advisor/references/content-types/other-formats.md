# Other Formats: Workshops, Videos, Syllabi, Rubrics, Course Design

This file covers educational content that doesn't fit cleanly into the other
content-type files but comes up often: workshops, video lessons not meant to
be watched straight through, rubrics as standalone artifacts, syllabi, course
designs, training modules, onboarding materials, study guides.

The key move here is to recognize which of the other content-type files is
closest, and supplement with the specific considerations below. Most of
these formats borrow heavily from lectures, tutorials, or assignments.

---

## Workshops (half-day, full-day, multi-day intensives)

A workshop is a lecture + lab + feedback session compressed into a high-intensity
experience, usually with professional or advanced-student learners who chose
to be there.

**Closest content-type overlaps**: `lectures.md` for the didactic segments,
`tutorials.md` for the hands-on segments, `homework-assignments.md` for any
exercises between/after.

**Specific considerations**:

- *Energy management*: attention budgets deplete fast. Plan modes that alternate —
  passive → active → constructive → interactive. Don't stack two long didactic
  segments back-to-back.
- *The 90-minute rule*: avoid segments longer than ~90 minutes without a break.
  Most workshops under-do breaks.
- *Objectives for adult learners*: connect to what they're already doing and
  about to do. Abstract objectives die fast here.
- *Followup matters disproportionately*: most workshop learning erodes fast
  without some planned consolidation. Build in a 1-week, 1-month followup of
  some kind — even just an email with a retrieval prompt.
- *Psychological safety in small groups*: workshops often put strangers
  together to do novel, difficult things. Investing time in framing,
  introductions, and norms pays off.

**Which theory files to read**:
- `practices/icap-engagement.md` (central — workshops should push to
  constructive/interactive)
- `principles/four-pillars.md` (attention is the binding constraint)
- `motivation/self-determination.md` (autonomy support matters more with
  adult learners)
- Plus whichever content-type files map to the segments

**For tactical implementation**, these `practical/` files are essential:
- `practical/discussion-and-questions.md` for facilitation of group conversation
- `practical/group-work.md` for hands-on/group segments
- `practical/formative-feedback.md` for mid-workshop pulse checks and
  muddiest-point moves that let you course-correct before the day ends
- `practical/lecture-delivery.md` for didactic segments

## Recorded video lessons

Video lessons that aren't meant as a recording of a live lecture but as their
own designed artifact — educational YouTube, MOOC video, screen-recorded
walkthrough, explainer animation.

**Closest content-type overlap**: `lectures.md` plus heavy use of
`cognition/multimedia-learning.md`.

**Specific considerations**:

- *Segmenting principle* matters a lot. Long videos should be chunked into
  pieces of ~6 minutes or less, each with its own focus and a natural stopping
  point.
- *Pre-training principle*: if a video introduces many new terms, introducing
  the terms first (in text or a separate intro segment) before using them
  reduces load.
- *Personalization principle*: conversational register ("you" and "we") out-
  performs formal register for learning outcomes, provided it doesn't become
  cringe.
- *Embodiment principle*: seeing a human gesturing and speaking generally helps
  (for emotional and attentional reasons) more than a disembodied voice.
  Voice-only works too, especially if the visual channel is already busy.
- *Signaling*: highlight what matters — visually, verbally, through pacing.
- *Modality*: auditory narration + visual diagrams beats auditory narration +
  on-screen text of the same words (redundancy).
- *Embedded checks*: a video lesson without embedded questions is almost
  purely passive. Even simple "pause here and try this" prompts pay off if
  they're followed.
- *Captions and transcripts*: default, not optional.
- *Accessibility*: audio descriptions where needed, good color contrast,
  readable fonts, no information conveyed only by color.

**Which theory files to read**:
- `cognition/multimedia-learning.md` (primary)
- `cognition/cognitive-load-theory.md`
- `practices/icap-engagement.md` (because videos are otherwise all-passive)
- `design/universal-design.md`

## Syllabi and course designs

A syllabus isn't just a policy document. It's the course's pedagogical
promise — learning objectives, structure, rhythm, assessment.

**Closest content-type overlap**: `homework-assignments.md` for the assignment
structure; `exams-and-quizzes.md` for the assessment plan; but the syllabus
as a whole is most related to backward design.

**Specific considerations**:

- *Backward design alignment*: start the review/design from objectives → how
  they're assessed → how learners are prepared to pass those assessments.
  Syllabi are often organized topically rather than along this line, and it
  shows.
- *Cognitive-load across the term*: does the pace give learners time to
  consolidate between peaks, or are the midterm and final in weeks 6 and 7?
- *Spacing and interleaving across weeks*: are concepts revisited in later
  weeks, or covered once and dropped?
- *Feedback loops*: are formative checkpoints built in? (low-stakes quizzes,
  drafts, in-class exercises.) How often does a learner find out whether
  their understanding is accurate?
- *Motivation structure*: how does early success get built? Is the hardest
  content in week 2, before any confidence has been earned?
- *Inclusive policies*: late work, participation, accommodations — does the
  syllabus frame these in ways that support learners or in ways designed to
  deter abuse?
- *Growth mindset signaling*: does the syllabus language signal "some of you
  can do this, most won't" or "with effort and good strategies, you can do
  this"?

**Which theory files to read**:
- `design/backward-design.md` (central)
- `design/learning-objectives.md`
- `practices/effective-techniques.md` (for spacing/interleaving across weeks)
- `motivation/mindset-expectancy.md` (for framing)
- `design/universal-design.md` (for inclusive policies)

**For the syllabus as a concrete document** — what to include, how to
structure it, first-day use, how it sets up the whole term — load
**`practical/course-and-syllabus.md`** as the primary practical file.
If the syllabus includes grading and academic-integrity policies, also
load `practical/grading-and-integrity.md`.

## Rubrics (as standalone artifacts)

A rubric is a rendering of the criteria used to judge student work. When
well-designed, a rubric is itself a teaching tool — it makes expectations
visible to students and lets them self-assess.

**Closest content-type overlap**: `exams-and-quizzes.md` and
`assessment/assessment-principles.md`.

**Specific considerations**:

- *Alignment*: the criteria should be derivable from the learning objectives.
  If the assignment objective is "develop a defensible argument with
  evidence," a criterion like "uses proper grammar" is weak alignment at
  best, unless grammar is itself an objective.
- *Criterion clarity*: each criterion is one dimension. Conflated criteria
  ("clarity and organization") are hard to apply consistently.
- *Level descriptors*: each level has concrete descriptors, not vague
  gradations. "Proficient: the argument is supported by at least three
  pieces of evidence from course materials, each properly cited" is
  applicable; "Proficient: the argument is well-supported" is not.
- *Weighting*: the weights should reflect what matters. If critical thinking
  is worth more than formatting, the rubric should say so.
- *Useable by both graders and students*: can two graders apply it
  consistently? Can a student use it to self-assess before submitting?
- *Analytic vs. holistic*: both have places. Analytic (criterion-by-criterion
  scoring) gives more diagnostic feedback; holistic (single overall rating
  against a description) is faster and often sufficient for formative use.
- *Avoids gaming*: rubrics that explicitly count small things (word counts,
  citations) can be gamed without improving substance. Balance concrete
  criteria with substantive ones.

**Which theory files to read**:
- `assessment/assessment-principles.md` (primary — especially the rubric section)
- `design/learning-objectives.md` (for alignment)
- `practices/feedback.md` (for how rubric-based feedback works)
- `design/bloom-taxonomy.md` (for criterion levels)

**For practical application** — how to calibrate multiple graders with the
rubric, handle disputes, communicate it to students — load
`practical/grading-and-integrity.md`.

## Training modules (corporate, compliance, onboarding)

Online training modules in workplace contexts — compliance, software
onboarding, soft-skills training. These are often badly designed but high-
volume, and small improvements have large aggregate effect.

**Closest content-type overlap**: `tutorials.md` for hands-on software training,
`textbook-chapters.md` for informational modules, `lectures.md` + `videos`
for narrative-driven modules.

**Specific considerations**:

- *Relevance crisis*: learners are often required to take these and perceive
  them as irrelevant. Addressing utility-value upfront is critical.
- *Just-in-time over comprehensive*: workplace training that covers everything
  someone might ever need is typically inferior to targeted, context-relevant
  micro-modules delivered at the moment of need.
- *Retrieval over recognition*: most compliance modules are click-through
  with recognition-based checks. Even adding a few real retrieval items
  would dramatically improve retention.
- *Scenarios and cases*: concrete scenarios outperform abstract rules.
  Design around the situations the learner will face.
- *Microlearning format*: short (3-7 min) modules with clear focus tend to
  outperform long ones.
- *Spaced review*: one-and-done training is famous for producing near-zero
  retention 30 days out. Build in spaced followup.
- *Accessibility and mobile*: often viewed on phones, often by people with
  disabilities. Don't treat this as afterthought.

**Which theory files to read**:
- `practices/effective-techniques.md` (retrieval, spacing, interleaving —
  workplace training is where these are most often ignored and most easily added)
- `motivation/self-determination.md` (utility-value, autonomy-supportive framing)
- `cognition/multimedia-learning.md`
- `design/universal-design.md`

## Study guides and review materials

Materials learners use to prepare for exams or consolidate knowledge: study
guides, review sheets, summary notes, flashcards, "cheat sheets."

**Closest content-type overlap**: a mix — part `textbook-chapters.md`,
part `exams-and-quizzes.md` (for the practice questions in them), part
`homework-assignments.md` (for the practice problems).

**Specific considerations**:

- *Retrieval over summary*: a review sheet that just compresses the material
  supports recognition-based studying, which is low-utility. Study guides
  with questions the learner answers from memory — or prompts that trigger
  retrieval — are much more effective.
- *Spaced and interleaved*: the best study guides organize around spaced
  revisit and interleaved practice rather than topic-by-topic review.
- *Self-explanation prompts*: "Why does the formula work?" "In your own
  words, explain…" Prompts to self-explain make study guides more than
  memorization tools.
- *Worked examples fading to independent*: for problem-solving subjects,
  a progression from worked examples → partially worked → independent is
  far more useful than "here's 50 problems."
- *Concept map vs. linear*: concept maps have mixed evidence (better than
  re-reading, worse than practice testing per Dunlosky) but they serve as
  useful scaffolds for identifying relationships.
- *Metacognitive prompts*: "Can you explain this without looking?" "Which
  topics do you feel least confident about?" Build self-monitoring into
  the guide.

**Which theory files to read**:
- `practices/effective-techniques.md` (primary — this is the file)
- `principles/four-pillars.md` (consolidation)
- `cognition/cognitive-load-theory.md` (for worked-example progressions)

## Office hours, advising, and one-on-one mentoring

Teaching that happens outside the classroom — in your office, in
hallways, by email, in individual conferences — has disproportionately
large effects on student outcomes relative to how little time it
consumes, yet is often the most-neglected part of instructional design.

**Closest content-type overlap**: none, really. Office hours and
advising are their own category.

**Specific considerations**:

- *Research is unambiguous*: frequency of informal faculty-student
  contact is one of the strongest predictors of retention,
  satisfaction, and achievement (Chickering & Gamson, 1991; Pascarella
  & Terenzini, 1991). Effect is largest for first-gen students and
  underrepresented groups — who are also least likely to come without
  explicit invitation.
- *Autonomy-supportive framing*: advising students is teaching them to
  reason through decisions, not making decisions for them.
- *Proactive outreach* to struggling students is effective and
  underused — a short email before disengagement sets in often changes
  trajectories.
- *Scheduling* matters — hours should be at times students can
  actually come, with explicit invitation, kept reliably.

**Which theory files to read**:
- `practical/office-hours-and-advising.md` (primary — the whole file)
- `motivation/self-determination.md` (for autonomy-supportive advising)
- `practices/feedback.md` (one-on-one is feedback)

## The meta-move: when something doesn't fit any of these

If the user brings something that really doesn't fit any content-type, ask
yourself three questions:

1. **What is the learner doing with this artifact?** Reading? Watching?
   Working through? Being judged by? Looking up? The answer points at the
   closest content-type.

2. **What learning outcome is this meant to produce?** Understanding? Skill
   acquisition? Behavioral change? Certification of prior learning? That
   points at which theory files are most relevant.

3. **Where in the broader learning experience does this fit?** First exposure,
   initial practice, spaced review, consolidation, assessment? Different
   stages benefit from different pedagogical moves.

With those answers, map to whatever combination of content-type and theory
files best fits, and apply the same standards: evidence-grounded, theory-named,
prioritized, honest. The specific format matters less than the underlying
learning science.
