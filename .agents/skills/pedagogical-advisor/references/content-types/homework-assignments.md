# Homework and Practice Assignments

This file covers assignments that students work on outside of (or in addition
to) direct instruction: problem sets, practice exercises, coding assignments,
lab write-ups, essays, projects, reading responses. The artifact can be a single
assignment or a sequence across a course.

Homework is where a lot of learning actually happens — or doesn't. A lecture
can be beautifully delivered and still produce little learning if the practice
doesn't work. This is where retrieval practice, spacing, interleaving, and
worked-example-to-independent-practice progression do their real work.

## What makes a homework assignment work

1. **Tight alignment to learning objectives.** Every significant question or
   task exercises something the objectives said learners should be able to do.
   Nothing important is missing; nothing unimportant is padding.
2. **Calibrated difficulty.** Hard enough to require real cognitive work
   (desirable difficulty) but not so hard that the learner can't gain traction
   (demotivating; self-efficacy damage). A common rule of thumb: the assignment
   is at the right level if a well-prepared student gets 80–85% right while
   actually thinking.
3. **Exercises retrieval, not recognition.** Learners are asked to produce
   answers from memory or reasoning, not pick from pre-specified options
   (unless recognition is the explicit goal).
4. **Worked-example-to-independent-practice progression.** For novel skills,
   early items are supported (worked or partially worked); later items expect
   independence. A problem set that's all at one scaffolding level teaches
   less than one that fades support.
5. **Spacing across the course.** Important material appears not only in the
   assignment after it's introduced but in subsequent assignments — so the
   learner revisits it over weeks, not only in one sitting.
6. **Interleaving of concept types.** When multiple related skills have been
   taught, the assignment mixes them rather than blocking each. Interleaving
   builds the discrimination that transfer requires.
7. **Meaningful feedback mechanism.** Graded or auto-graded, with feedback
   that goes beyond "right/wrong" to address process and strategy where
   appropriate. Feedback arrives in a window where the learner can still
   act on it.
8. **Reasonable workload.** The time-on-task estimate is honest. Chronically
   overlong assignments degrade learning because learners start satisficing.
9. **Clear expectations.** The learner knows what good looks like — via
   rubrics, examples, or explicit criteria. Opaque expectations punish
   low-prior-knowledge and first-generation students disproportionately.
10. **Motivating framing.** The utility, relevance, or intrinsic interest
    of the task is visible — not just "do problems 4.1-4.15."

## The most common problems

- **Misalignment with objectives.** The course teaches conceptual
  understanding; the homework tests procedural fluency (or vice versa). The
  assessment doesn't match the instruction.
- **Blocked practice only.** All the problems are the same type, in a row,
  from the chapter that was just covered. Looks efficient, builds shallow
  performance, fails transfer. (See Dunlosky on interleaved practice.)
- **No retrieval.** The assignment is essentially "re-read and regurgitate"
  — fill in blanks that are obvious from context, or match items to definitions
  you just read. This uses recognition rather than recall and misses the
  testing effect.
- **Too many novel elements per problem.** Early practice problems throw
  every concept from the chapter at once. Learners who haven't built
  individual schemas can't cope.
- **Worked-example-free zone.** The chapter teaches a technique, and the
  homework jumps straight to independent practice with no worked or
  partial-worked examples. Novices struggle; only the pre-prepared get
  through.
- **No progression.** Items 1-15 are all at the same level. There's no
  gradient from simpler to harder, no "reach" items, no way for a learner to
  stop when they've demonstrated mastery.
- **Ambiguous rubrics or none at all.** The assignment is graded on "quality"
  or "effort" with no concrete criteria. Learners can't self-assess and
  instructors can't be consistent.
- **Graded on performance, not learning.** Especially damaging for novice
  learners: high-stakes grading on homework discourages trying hard things
  and experimenting. Practice should often be low-stakes.
- **Stale across a course.** Homework for week 5 exercises only week 5
  material. By week 10, week 2 content has been forgotten. Spacing is absent.
- **Plagiarism-design vs. learning-design.** The assignment is built around
  preventing cheating rather than building skill. The result is tasks that are
  annoying rather than formative.
- **AI-era blind spot.** Assignments written before generative AI that have
  effectively zero cognitive demand on the learner now because any reasonably
  competent LLM does them.
- **Opaque time cost.** Nominal "2-hour" assignment that actually takes 8.
  Learners burn out or cut corners. Either way, learning suffers.
- **No followup.** Assignment is handed in, graded, handed back — and never
  revisited. Misconceptions visible in the submission aren't corrected and
  persist into the next assignment.

## Diagnostic checklist

**Alignment and objectives**
- Is it clear which learning objectives each section/item exercises?
- Does the full assignment cover the objectives fully, or are there gaps?
- Is anything in the assignment *not* serving an objective (padding)?
- Is the Bloom level of the items matched to the course's stated level?
  (Asking "define X" when the objective is "apply X" is a misalignment.)
  → `design/backward-design.md`, `design/learning-objectives.md`,
  `design/bloom-taxonomy.md`

**Difficulty calibration and progression**
- Is there a visible gradient — first items more supported / simpler, later
  items more independent / harder?
- Are there worked or partially-worked examples for novel procedural skills,
  or does the assignment jump to independent practice immediately?
- Can a well-prepared student complete most of it while actually thinking?
  (If it's trivial or impossible, calibration is off.)
- Are there stretch items for advanced learners — or is the assignment
  flat?
  → `cognition/cognitive-load-theory.md`, `motivation/mindset-expectancy.md`

**Retrieval, spacing, interleaving**
- Does the assignment require *retrieval* (producing answers from memory /
  reasoning) rather than only *recognition*?
- If it's a multi-week course, does this assignment include material from
  prior weeks, or only the most recent material? (Spacing.)
- When multiple related concepts are in play, does the assignment mix them
  or block them? (Interleaving.)
- Are items varied enough that the learner has to decide *which* approach to
  apply, or is the approach pre-cued by context?
  → `practices/effective-techniques.md`

**Engagement mode**
- Using ICAP: does the assignment require *constructive* work (producing
  something in one's own words, explaining, justifying, generating) or only
  *active* (applying a procedure)?
- Are there self-explanation prompts? ("Explain your reasoning." "Why does
  this step work?") These are highly effective when well-designed.
- Is there a collaborative element where appropriate, and is it designed to
  require real interaction rather than parallel work?
  → `practices/icap-engagement.md`

**Clarity and expectations**
- Is it clear what a good submission looks like? Rubric, examples, or
  explicit criteria?
- Is the task statement unambiguous, or is it going to generate a flood of
  "what did you mean by…" questions?
- Is the time estimate honest? Has anyone tested it with the target audience?
- For coding or technical assignments, is the starter code / environment
  clear and low-friction?
  → `assessment/assessment-principles.md` (for rubrics),
  `design/universal-design.md`

**Feedback mechanism**
- What feedback does the learner get, when, and on what?
- Does the feedback address process and strategy, or only task correctness?
- Is there a mechanism for the learner to act on feedback — revisions,
  followup problems, discussion?
- Is the assignment graded in a way that encourages learning (low-stakes,
  formative) or performance (high-stakes, summative)? And is this the right
  choice for its place in the course?
  → `practices/feedback.md`

**Motivation and relevance**
- Is the *why* of the assignment visible? Relevance to the learner's goals,
  career, interests?
- For longer projects: does the learner have any autonomy — choice of topic,
  approach, or framing?
- Does the framing support growth mindset — emphasizing development and
  process — or does it anchor to fixed ability?
- Is the difficulty such that success is earned but feasible, supporting
  self-efficacy?
  → `motivation/self-determination.md`, `motivation/mindset-expectancy.md`

**AI-era concerns** (where applicable)
- If a student runs this assignment through an LLM, what would be left?
  - If the answer is "not much" and the assignment isn't being run in a
    proctored environment, the assignment's learning value is at risk.
  - Usual remediation: shift toward process-focused artifacts (journals,
    iterative drafts with feedback), in-class components, verbal/oral
    checkpoints, or integrate AI use explicitly with reflection on it.
- Note: this isn't primarily about catching cheaters but about designing
  assignments whose cognitive work *still* has to be done by the learner.

**Workload and equity**
- Is the workload reasonable? Consistent with other assignments in the
  course? Appropriate for the credit load?
- Does the assignment disproportionately advantage students with certain
  prior experiences, background knowledge, hardware, internet access?
  → `design/universal-design.md`

   practice, spaced practice, interleaved practice, self-explanation are
   all directly relevant and often overlooked.

   of the tasks matches the stated objectives.

## Output format

For critique, use the standard Strengths → Critical → Important → Refinements
→ Tradeoffs → Next Steps format. Cite specific items (`[Problem 3]`,
`[Section B]`, `[Part 2, Question 4]`).

Particular things to check and call out:

- Objective coverage: which objectives are well-exercised, which are missed,
  which are over-weighted.
- Interleaving/spacing: whether the assignment and the sequence of assignments
  is designed for long-term retention, not only immediate performance.
- Difficulty curve: whether there's a gradient, and whether stretch items
  exist.
- Rubric clarity: whether a student or a grader could use the rubric
  consistently.

For design, structure as:

```
## Assignment title
## Learning objectives exercised
## Prerequisite knowledge
## Time estimate (honest)
## Assignment structure:
   - Part 1 (what it exercises, scaffolding level, cognitive mode)
   - Part 2 (…)
   - …
## Difficulty progression (how support fades)
## Items interleaved/spaced with prior material
## Rubric / success criteria
## Feedback mechanism and turnaround
## Followup (how will learners act on feedback? how will this material be
   revisited later?)
```

## A note on problem sets vs. projects

Problem sets (short-answer, repeated-skill practice) and projects (multi-step,
integrative, often open-ended) need different emphases.

- **Problem sets** benefit most from spacing, interleaving, retrieval practice,
  and worked-example-to-independent progression.
- **Projects** benefit most from clear learning objectives and rubrics,
  scaffolded milestones, autonomy support, formative feedback on drafts,
  and genuine challenge/relevance.

If the assignment is a project, lean on `motivation/self-determination.md`,
`assessment/assessment-principles.md` (for the rubric), and
`design/backward-design.md`.
