# Assessment Principles

Assessment is the point where design, instruction, and learner
experience are tested against reality. Good assessment measures what
it claims to measure, does so consistently, treats learners fairly,
and produces information useful to learners and instructors alike.

Bad assessment can invalidate everything else. A course can have
thoughtful objectives, well-designed materials, and engaged learners,
and still reach flawed conclusions about who learned what if the
assessment is wrong.

## Validity

**Validity** is the degree to which an assessment measures what it
claims to measure. It's the most important property of an assessment,
and the one most often compromised in practice.

Several kinds of validity are worth distinguishing:

### Construct validity

Does the assessment actually tap the underlying cognitive capacity
or understanding you're trying to measure? If the objective is "apply
a concept to novel situations," does the assessment actually require
application to novel situations — or does it allow success through
memorized procedures?

This is the most fundamental validity question. An assessment with
low construct validity measures something *other than* what it claims
to measure — often, some combination of reading speed, test-taking
savvy, familiarity with the instructor's idiosyncrasies, and the
construct you wanted.

Threats to construct validity:
- **Construct under-representation**: the assessment only taps part of
  the construct. A thesis-writing course whose assessment is only
  multiple choice on grammar under-represents thesis writing.
- **Construct-irrelevant variance**: factors other than the construct
  affect scores. Reading load on a math exam inflates for slow
  readers without reflecting math ability.

### Content validity

Does the assessment cover the domain of content the course is about,
and is the coverage proportional to the content's importance? A
history exam that only covers one unit of a six-unit course has
weak content validity even if the items are good.

Typically addressed through a **test blueprint** — a table mapping
objectives and topics to items, with proportional weight.

### Criterion validity

Do scores correlate with some external criterion they should
correlate with? Does performance on this assessment predict real-world
performance in the skill being assessed?

Harder to establish, but matters especially for high-stakes or
gateway assessments.

### Face validity

Does the assessment *look* valid to learners? Do they perceive it as
measuring what the course was about? Low face validity undermines
motivation even if other validity types are fine — learners
disengage from assessments they perceive as "tricks" or
"irrelevant."

## Reliability

**Reliability** is the consistency of measurement. If the same
learner's knowledge were measured on two occasions, or by two
graders, or by two forms of the assessment, how consistent would
scores be?

Reliability doesn't guarantee validity (you can consistently measure
the wrong thing). But unreliable assessments can't be valid — if
scores don't stabilize, they can't accurately measure anything.

### Sources of unreliability

- **Item noise**: random variation in how items function. A few items
  can produce wildly different scores if the items are flawed.
- **Inter-rater variability**: for constructed-response items,
  different graders give different scores to the same response.
  Mitigated by clear rubrics and calibration training.
- **Test length**: shorter assessments are inherently noisier. A
  single item per construct gives a very noisy subscore.
- **Test-taking conditions**: illness, anxiety, environmental factors.
  Harder to control but worth recognizing.

### Rough targets

- For professional/high-stakes testing, reliability coefficients
  (Cronbach's alpha or similar) of 0.9+ are typical targets.
- For classroom assessments, 0.7-0.8 is a reasonable target.
- Under 0.6, the assessment has substantial noise.

Most classroom assessments aren't psychometrically analyzed, but the
principles still apply: more items per construct = more reliable;
clearer rubrics = more reliable; better-written items = more reliable.

## Fairness

**Fairness** is distinct from validity and reliability, though
related. An assessment is fair when it provides comparable
opportunity for learners to demonstrate their knowledge, independent
of characteristics unrelated to the construct.

### Threats to fairness

- **Differential opportunity to learn**: some learners had access to
  better instruction, more practice, or more prep. If the assessment
  measures opportunity as much as learning, it's unfair.
- **Cultural or contextual bias**: items that require cultural
  background not part of the course content.
- **Language bias**: unnecessary linguistic complexity that
  disadvantages ESL or reading-disabled learners without being part
  of the construct.
- **Accommodation gaps**: learners with disabilities who don't get
  appropriate accommodations produce scores that don't reflect their
  knowledge.
- **Stereotype threat**: contextual factors that activate negative
  stereotypes can depress performance; demographic questions at the
  start of a test are one documented example.

## Formative vs. summative assessment

A distinction that cuts across validity, reliability, fairness:

**Summative assessment** is used to judge learning at an endpoint — a
final exam, a certification test, an end-of-unit project. Its
purpose is to measure what the learner has achieved. The
consequences for the learner (grade, credit, certification) are
central.

**Formative assessment** is used during learning to inform the
learning process. Its purpose is to produce information useful for
the next step — for the learner (what do I need to work on?) and the
instructor (where is my class collectively, where are the gaps?).

Both are valuable. They're not competitors. But they should be
designed differently:

### Formative assessment design

- Low stakes (or zero stakes). Grades undermine the formative
  purpose because they shift motivation from learning to
  grade-protection.
- Frequent and embedded, not episodic. Short, regular checks
  produce more learning than occasional long ones.
- Fast feedback. Formative information is only useful if it reaches
  the learner in time to act on it.
- Covers current material, not cumulative. Focused feedback on what
  was just learned.
- Can be less reliable (fewer items per construct) because the
  stakes are low.

### Summative assessment design

- Reliability matters more because consequences are attached.
- Cumulative where the course is cumulative.
- Validity critical — the summative judgment must reflect what the
  course was about.
- Clearer rubrics and criteria so that scores are defensible.
- Fairness built in — accommodations, accessibility, reasonable time.

### The frequent failure: summative without formative

A common pattern: a course has summative assessments (midterm,
final) but no real formative assessment. Learners don't know where
they stand until it's too late to act on the information.

Adding frequent low-stakes retrieval-practice quizzes — which are
both formative assessment and retrieval practice (Dunlosky
high-utility technique) — is among the highest-leverage interventions
in such courses.

## Assessment and learning

Assessment doesn't just measure learning; it *drives* learning.
Learners study what they think will be on the test. Instructors
teach toward what they'll test. This "washback" effect means:

- A test that rewards memorization will produce memorization-focused
  study, regardless of what the syllabus says.
- A test that requires application and analysis will produce
  application- and analysis-focused study.
- A course whose visible incentive structure conflicts with its
  stated objectives will have learners follow the incentive, not
  the objective.

**Implication**: the test defines the curriculum. If you want
learners to develop higher-order thinking, the assessments have to
require higher-order thinking.

## Rubrics

A rubric is a structured description of the criteria used to judge
performance, with specifications for what different quality levels
look like. Good rubrics serve multiple functions:

1. **Clarify expectations** for learners before they do the work.
2. **Support consistent grading** across items and graders.
3. **Provide structured feedback** aligned with the criteria.
4. **Enable self- and peer-assessment** because the criteria are
   visible.

### Types of rubrics

**Analytic rubrics** separate criteria — the assessment scores each
criterion separately, then combines them. Example: for an essay,
separate scores for thesis, evidence, organization, and prose.
Strengths: diagnostic feedback; alignment with multiple objectives.
Weaknesses: grading takes longer; risk of the parts adding up to
less than the whole.

**Holistic rubrics** give a single overall score against descriptive
levels. Example: 5 = exceeds expectations; 4 = meets; 3 = approaching;
etc. Strengths: fast; captures overall quality. Weaknesses: less
diagnostic.

**Single-point rubrics** describe only the target level (what
proficient looks like), with space for comments on what exceeded
or fell short. Strengths: simple, growth-focused. Weaknesses:
less support for standardization.

Choose based on purpose. Formative assessment often benefits from
analytic rubrics (diagnostic). Simpler assignments may be fine with
holistic. Complex multi-criterion work needs analytic.

### Rubric quality

A rubric is useful if:

- **Each criterion is distinct** — not overlapping with other
  criteria.
- **Levels are concrete** — descriptors specify observable features,
  not just "good/fair/poor."
- **Weighting is explicit** and aligned with objectives.
- **Two graders using it would agree** reasonably often.
- **A student could use it to self-assess** before submitting.

Common rubric problems:
- Vague descriptors ("high quality," "well-organized") that rely on
  grader judgment.
- Overlapping criteria that lead to double-counting.
- Weighting that doesn't match stated priorities.
- Too many criteria to apply consistently.
- Criteria disconnected from learning objectives.

## Item analysis

For assessments that will be reused or refined, item analysis is the
process of examining how individual items functioned — whether they
discriminated well, whether distractors worked, whether the item
aligned with other items.

Basic item analysis statistics:

- **Item difficulty** (*p*): proportion of students who got it right.
  Too high (everyone got it) or too low (no one got it) are both
  problematic. For a typical norm-referenced test, targeting items in
  the 0.3-0.8 range is reasonable.
- **Item discrimination** (*D*): do higher-scoring students do
  better on this item than lower-scoring students? Positive
  discrimination is expected; near-zero or negative discrimination
  flags items that may be flawed.
- **Distractor analysis** (for MCQs): which distractors are chosen
  and by whom? Distractors never chosen are ineffective; distractors
  chosen more by high-scorers than low-scorers suggest confusing
  items.

Even without formal item analysis, instructors can gain a lot by
skimming the patterns of who got what right after scoring.

## Grading vs. assessing

A distinction worth making:

**Assessing** is gathering information about learning.
**Grading** is translating assessment information into a single
summary judgment (a grade, a score, a designation).

These aren't the same. You can assess without grading (formative
quizzes, drafts, practice). You can grade without really assessing
(pure effort or attendance grades; grades disconnected from
objectives).

Common conflations:
- Using grades as the primary assessment tool. Learning information is
  diluted by the grading function.
- Avoiding all grading to "focus on learning." In credit-bearing
  contexts, learners typically need some grade structure; pretending
  otherwise doesn't serve them.
- Grading effort rather than outcome, without clear objectives for
  what effort was supposed to produce.

The useful move: be explicit about which assessments are formative
(information, no stakes) and which are summative (judgment, stakes),
and design each for its purpose.

## Some alternative assessment approaches

Beyond traditional tests and papers:

- **Portfolio assessment**: cumulative collection of work showing
  development over time. Valuable for creative work, writing,
  complex project-based learning. Needs clear criteria.
- **Performance assessment / authentic assessment**: learners
  demonstrate capability through tasks that resemble real-world
  application. Often the most valid for applied skills.
- **Mastery-based assessment**: learners move forward when they've
  mastered content, rather than on a schedule. Re-takes and revision
  built in.
- **Oral examinations / defense**: one-on-one conversation probing
  understanding. High face validity, strong at revealing depth;
  expensive to scale.
- **Contract grading**: students contract for a target grade by
  committing to do specified work at specified quality. Shifts
  motivation structure.

Each has its own validity/reliability/fairness characteristics. None
is universally superior.

## Critique checklist

When reviewing an assessment plan or specific assessments:

### Validity
- Does the assessment tap the cognitive capacity the objective
  specifies?
- Is the content coverage proportional to course priorities?
- Is construct-irrelevant variance minimized (reading load, format
  familiarity, cultural assumptions)?

### Reliability
- Are there enough items per construct?
- Are rubrics specific enough for consistent grading?
- Is the scoring process replicable?

### Fairness
- Does the assessment give comparable opportunity across learners?
- Are accommodations built in?
- Is linguistic complexity calibrated to the audience?

### Formative/summative balance
- Are there formative checkpoints throughout, or only summative
  endpoints?
- Does the grading structure support or undermine formative purposes?

### Alignment
- Does the assessment match the stated learning objectives?
- Is the Bloom level of the items appropriate to the objectives?
- Is there a test blueprint or similar alignment document?

### Washback
- What will the assessment push learners to study?
- Does that match what the course claims to value?

## Summary

**An assessment is only useful insofar as it provides valid,
reliable, and fair information about what learners have learned —
and produces that information in a way that supports further
learning.**

**Validity**: does it measure what it claims?
**Reliability**: is the measurement consistent?
**Fairness**: does it give comparable opportunity to all learners?

**Formative and summative serve different purposes** — design for
each accordingly.

**Rubrics clarify** expectations, support consistency, and enable
self-assessment. Quality rubric design matters as much as quality
item design.

**Assessment drives what learners study and what instructors
teach.** Aligning assessment with objectives isn't nice-to-have;
it's the core of a coherent course.
