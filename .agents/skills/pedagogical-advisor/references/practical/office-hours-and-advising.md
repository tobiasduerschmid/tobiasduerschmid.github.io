# Office Hours and Advising

Teaching doesn't happen only in the classroom. Office hours, one-on-
one conversations, and informal advising turn out to have
disproportionately large effects on student outcomes — retention,
satisfaction, achievement, and learning — relative to how little time
they consume. This is probably the most under-leveraged part of most
instructors' teaching.

Most of the skill's other files address classroom teaching. This one
addresses the teaching that happens in your office, in hallways, via
email, and in ten-minute conversations after class.

Drawn from Davis's *Tools for Teaching* chapters 44 (Holding Office
Hours) and 45 (Academic Advising and Mentoring Undergraduates), with
supporting research on student-faculty contact from Chickering &
Gamson and Pascarella & Terenzini.

## Why Office Hours Matter More Than They Look Like They Matter

Research converges on a finding that surprises most faculty: the
frequency of informal, out-of-classroom contact with faculty is one
of the strongest predictors of student retention, satisfaction, and
achievement (Chickering & Gamson, 1991; Pascarella & Terenzini,
1991). The effect is largest for first-generation students,
underrepresented minorities, and students with weaker academic
backgrounds — exactly the students least likely to come to office
hours without explicit invitation.

Office hours do several things classroom time can't:
- **Personalize the instructor-student relationship**, converting
  the "anonymous authority" into "a specific person I've met"
- **Surface confusion** that students won't expose in front of peers
- **Reveal struggling students early** enough to help
- **Signal that the instructor cares** about individual students
- **Calibrate the instructor's sense** of how the course is landing

Marincovich and Rusk: "Students are often motivated to work harder
for teachers they have come to know."

## Setting Up Office Hours

### Scheduling

Two to four hours a week is typical. Vary days and times so
different student schedules can fit. Avoid back-to-back blocks in a
single 4-hour marathon — spreading reduces the likelihood that a
given student finds all your hours conflict with their classes.

Offer a non-obvious option: instead of M 10–12, try M 9–10, T 3–4,
Th 9–10. Or 9:30–10:30 so students with either 9am or 10am classes
can come.

Always add: "and by appointment." Some students' schedules genuinely
don't fit; make the alternative available.

### Communication

In the syllabus, on the classroom whiteboard the first day, outside
your office door, and mentioned in class before major deadlines.
Students — especially new ones — may not know what office hours are
*for*. Say explicitly: "You can come to ask about the material, to
review a graded assignment, to talk about what we covered in class,
to discuss further reading, or to talk about the field more broadly.
You don't need a specific question — curiosity is welcome."

### Discipline

**Keep your office hours.** If you must miss one, post a note (on
the door, via email, on the course site). Students who arrive to a
missing instructor experience it as disregard, and it undermines
their willingness to come again. Davis: "Students get upset with
instructors who are not present for the full period of their posted
office hours, and these feelings can impair their motivation to do
the course work."

## Encouraging Students to Come

Most students don't come to office hours voluntarily, especially in
the first month. Counter-moves:

**Issue explicit invitations, repeatedly.** Not once in the syllabus
but in class, before exams, on returned work.

**Make at least one office hour visit mandatory early in the term.**
In smaller classes, schedule each student for a 10–20 minute
appointment in the first 3–4 weeks. Purpose: get to know them, find
out their background, identify early concerns. Once students have
been to your office once, the second visit is vastly easier.

**Return graded work with "Please see me"** for students who did
poorly or for particularly promising work. Davis cites a 75%
response rate to that comment. Use it sparingly so it retains
signal.

**Use office hours as an orientation** for students who joined
late. This solves a real problem (they missed the first day) and
gives them a reason to come.

**Post interesting material near your office door** — problem
answers, a paper you liked, a new journal article. It draws
passers-by.

**Hold group office hours** before major deadlines or exams —
announce a topic ("review of Chapter 5") and expect groups, not
individuals.

## Conducting Office Hours

### Physical setup

A closed door feels bureaucratic. Keep it open. Sit beside or in
front of the desk rather than behind it — the desk as barrier
increases the "going to the principal" feel.

### Opening the conversation

Let the student name the purpose: "What can I do for you?" or "What
brings you by?" Don't prescribe the agenda — let them tell you what
they need.

### Attention

Give undivided attention. Put aside papers. Let calls go to
voicemail. Students often fear they're wasting the instructor's
time; visible focus counters that fear and converts a transactional
visit into a relational one.

### Handling substance

**For content questions**: have the student articulate their current
understanding first. "Walk me through what you've tried / what you
think is going on." Their articulation usually reveals the sticking
point faster than your explanation would.

**For quantitative problems**: don't just give the answer. Work it
through aloud, showing the reasoning. Let the student try the next
similar problem. Leave them with something they can study later.

**For broad "I don't understand Chapter 9" questions**: push back
gently. "What specifically in Chapter 9 stumped you?" Students who
can't localize their confusion often haven't read carefully; sending
them back to do that is kinder than trying to re-teach the chapter.

**For the "is this right?" question about their own work**: resist
answering directly. Suggest a way they can check for themselves.
Teaching the self-checking move matters more than the single answer.

### Multiple students at once

If two arrive with similar questions, introduce them to each other
and let one explain to the other while you listen in. The
explainer consolidates; the listener gets peer-voiced explanation
(often clearer than faculty-voiced). When busy, group students
productively rather than queue them.

### Boundaries

**Personal problems**: students sometimes bring non-academic issues.
Express concern, listen briefly, but refer to counseling services.
You're not a therapist, and acting as one serves neither of you
well.

**Time**: if students arrive five minutes before the hour ends, be
honest: "I have only a few minutes — what's the most pressing
question?" Invite them back.

**Availability creep**: some students would expand office hours
indefinitely. Hold the line on the stated hours while offering
alternatives (email, appointment, referral). Burnout serves no one.

## Academic Advising

For faculty in formal advising roles, or for any instructor who's
asked "what courses should I take next semester?"

### Know the stakes

Advising is not casual bureaucratic sign-off. Research (Metzner,
Voorhees) shows good advising substantially improves student
retention — and that even poor advising outperforms no advising.
The frequency of faculty-student interaction outside class predicts
retention, satisfaction, and success.

### Know your stuff

Department and institutional requirements, prerequisite structures,
major policies, referral offices, major-to-career paths. You
don't have to memorize everything, but know where to look. A wrong
advising answer delivered confidently is worse than "I don't know,
but let me find out."

### Structure the conversation

**Let the student name the purpose.** Then cover:
- What they're trying to accomplish (major, career, interests)
- What they've done so far
- What's next in their program
- What questions they have

**Take notes.** In a file for each advisee, record dates of
meetings, topics discussed, decisions reached. Skim before next
meeting. Continuity signals care.

### The three checkpoints

Davis (citing Halgin & Halgin) recommends three required meetings
at minimum:
- **Summer before first year** — transition support
- **Spring of sophomore year** — major declaration, direction
- **Fall of senior year** — post-graduation planning

Add more for struggling students, pre-professional students with
specific requirements, or students whose interests are shifting.

### Autonomy-supportive framing

Don't make decisions *for* students. Help them reason through them.
("Here are the considerations. What do you think matters most?")
This matches the SDT finding (see `motivation/self-determination.md`)
that autonomy supports motivation. A student who chose their path
with your support owns it; a student who followed your directive
resents it.

### Confidentiality

Know your institution's policy on student records. In the U.S.,
FERPA limits what you can share. Grades and progress generally
cannot be discussed with parents without written student consent.

### When to refer

Beyond academic advising, students may need:
- Counseling services (emotional/mental health)
- Disability services (accommodations)
- Financial aid office
- Career services
- Writing center, tutoring, math center
- International student office

Know the contacts on your campus. Refer explicitly — give the
office name, location, and contact info — rather than vague "you
should talk to someone."

## Mentoring Beyond Advising

For students you've taught well, mentoring extends past the course.

**Write good letters of recommendation.** Specific, calibrated,
comparing the student to peer groups you've seen. Request context
from the student — what they're applying for, what aspects you
should emphasize — before drafting.

**Invite continued contact.** Keep former students informed of
relevant opportunities, research, job openings. The network matters;
so does the demonstrated care.

**Recommend concrete next steps.** Research experiences, reading,
specific follow-up courses, professional organizations. Vague
encouragement ("you should go to grad school") is less useful than
pointed suggestions ("you should look at program X; I know someone
there").

## Struggling Students

Some patterns that warrant proactive outreach:
- Grades dropping mid-term
- Missed assignments or exams without contact
- Withdrawn or disengaged affect in class
- Dramatic change in pattern from earlier in term

A short email — "I noticed you missed the last two classes. Is
everything okay? I'd be happy to help you catch up" — often matters
disproportionately. Students who feel invisible when struggling
often don't come back; students who feel noticed often do.

## What To Look For When Critiquing Faculty-Student Contact Design

### Office hours
- Are they scheduled at varied, accessible times?
- Are they clearly communicated?
- Are they kept reliably?
- Is there explicit encouragement beyond the syllabus mention?
- Is there a mechanism for students to come at least once?

### Advising (if applicable)
- Are check-ins structured, or only on request?
- Is there note-taking for continuity?
- Are students treated as decision-makers with support, or
  directed?
- Are referrals concrete (office name, contact) or vague?

### Proactive outreach
- Is there a mechanism for identifying struggling students early?
- Does the instructor reach out, or wait for students to seek
  help?

## Red Flags

- **Posted office hours that are never actually kept** (or kept
  inconsistently) — erodes trust rapidly.
- **Single 2-hour block at a time students are universally in
  class** — technically meeting policy, functionally unavailable.
- **No explicit invitation in class** — relying on the syllabus
  alone.
- **Instructor visibly inconvenienced when students come** — the
  behavior reaches students and reduces future visits.
- **Advising that's purely form-signing** with no conversation.
- **No outreach to students who disappear** — silent failure
  tolerated as student responsibility.
- **Personal problems dismissed rather than referred.**

## Summary

**Office hours are one of the highest-leverage uses of instructor
time**, and most faculty underuse them.

**The barrier is almost always the student's, not the instructor's.**
Explicit invitation, mandatory early contact, welcoming setup, and
reliable hours dramatically increase utilization.

**Advising is not bureaucratic sign-off** — it's one of the
strongest predictors of student retention and success. Treat it
as teaching.

**Support autonomy, don't direct.** Students own decisions they
reasoned to; they resent decisions imposed on them.

**Proactive outreach to struggling students is effective and
under-used.** A short email often matters disproportionately.
