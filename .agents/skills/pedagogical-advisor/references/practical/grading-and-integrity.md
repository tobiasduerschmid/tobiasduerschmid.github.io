# Grading Practices and Academic Integrity

`assessment/assessment-principles.md` covers the psychometrics —
validity, reliability, rubric design. This file covers the practical
craft of grading: what actually to grade, how to do it consistently
at scale, how to defuse disputes, how to calculate final grades, and
how to structure a course so academic dishonesty is rare rather than
endemic.

Drawn from Davis's *Tools for Teaching* chapters 32 (Grading
Practices), 33 (Calculating and Assigning Grades), and 34
(Preventing Academic Dishonesty).

## The Purpose of Grading

Grades serve several distinct purposes, and problems arise when they
serve purposes they weren't designed for.

Scriven's (1974) six functions of grading, per Davis:
- Describe the worth, merit, or value of work accomplished
- Improve students' ability to evaluate their own work
- Stimulate and encourage good work
- Communicate the teacher's judgment of student progress
- Inform the teacher about what students have and haven't learned
- Select people for rewards or continued education

**The foundational rule**: grade on the basis of students' mastery
of knowledge and skills — not on participation, attendance, effort,
attitude, or personality. When these non-academic factors count
toward a grade, the grade loses its meaning as an indicator of
learning. Reward attendance and effort through other means (bonus
points, narrative feedback, encouragement) rather than folding them
into the grade.

## Grading Structures

### Criterion-referenced vs. norm-referenced

**Criterion-referenced** ("absolute") grading: students earn grades
by meeting specified standards. Anyone who meets the A standard earns
an A; if everyone meets it, everyone earns an A.

**Norm-referenced** ("curve") grading: grades distribute across a
predetermined curve regardless of absolute performance. A fixed
percentage earn each grade.

Davis (citing Crooks, McKeachie) is explicit: **avoid grading on the
curve in most contexts.** Curves:
- Discourage collaboration (your classmate's success lowers yours)
- Produce anxiety that interferes with learning
- Reduce motivation to use feedback — why improve if your grade
  depends on relative position?
- Create arbitrary distinctions at boundaries

Curves are defensible in a few narrow cases (highly variable exam
difficulty, forced selection for a limited slot), but as a default
course policy they actively undermine learning.

### Grade components

Most courses should have **multiple grade components**, not one or
two:
- Regular low-stakes assessments (quizzes, problem sets)
- Mid-semester larger assessment (midterm, paper, project)
- Final larger assessment (final exam, capstone project)

**Why multiple components**:
- Reduces single-event risk (a student's bad day doesn't tank the grade)
- Provides ongoing information to students about their standing
- Gives the instructor multiple data points on each student
- Supports formative use — early grades guide studying for later ones

**Rule of thumb** (Erickson & Strommer, for lower-division courses):
some form of evaluation every 2–3 weeks.

### Weighting

Weights should reflect:
- Importance of the content area
- Reliability of the assessment (more reliable → can weight more)
- Cumulative vs. topical (final exam more cumulative → weight more)

State weights in the syllabus. Don't change them mid-course.

## Practical Grading Tactics

### Return the first graded assignment early

Return the first significant graded work *before* the add/drop
deadline. This lets students who are in over their heads reassess
before they're stuck. Some instructors let students drop or redo the
very first assessment — the logic is that students couldn't have
known the expectations until they saw the first one.

### Record numerically, not as letters

Record tests and assignments by point value, not letter grade.
Letters lose information (an 89 and a 91 both become some letter)
and make final calculation imprecise. Convert to letters only at the
end.

### Give students sense of their standing throughout

For each graded item, share:
- The student's score
- The distribution of scores (range, mean)
- What level of performance is satisfactory

Students shouldn't discover at finals week that they've been failing
since October.

### Allow rewrites where appropriate

For writing-intensive assignments, rewrites with grade improvement
(partial, not full) support learning. The student who rewrites
demonstrates they've processed the feedback — which is the point.
Some instructors average original and rewrite; some take the higher
grade with a small discount.

### If many students fail, consider re-teaching

If a large fraction of the class fails an exam, the signal is that
the teaching or preparation failed, not that the students did.
Davis's advice: rather than moving on, schedule a re-teach of the
troubled material and a second assessment. Students still have a
chance to master the content; the instructor learns what didn't work
the first time.

### Keep thorough records

Keep a gradebook with all component scores throughout the term. You
will need it for grade disputes, incompletes, and letters of
recommendation.

## Handling Grade Disputes

Grade disputes are uncomfortable but predictable. Handling them
badly erodes trust; handling them well reinforces it.

**Cool-off rule**: ask upset students to take at least a day before
discussing a grade. Emotions in the moment rarely lead to productive
conversation.

**Require written justification**: ask the student to write out
their specific objections and what they think the correct grade
should be. This forces articulation and often resolves the issue —
the student discovers on paper that their case is weaker than it
felt.

**When you meet**: have all relevant materials — the exam, the
scoring key, sample answers at different grade levels. Listen without
becoming antagonized. Explain the criteria. Show how the student's
response compares to the criteria.

**Be willing to change a grade if you were wrong.** Graders make
mistakes. Admitting one and adjusting is not weakness; defending a
mistake reflexively is.

**Don't change a grade because of pressure unrelated to performance**
— personal need (graduate school, dean's list) doesn't change what
the work demonstrated. If warranted, offer to write a supplementary
letter describing the student's work in context.

**Standardize across graders.** If TAs or graduate instructors are
involved, calibrate early. McKeachie's recommended procedure: meet
as a group, review model answers and scoring criteria, form 2–3-
person teams that independently score sample exams, compare and
discuss discrepancies until consensus, then grade independently.

## Calculating Final Grades

Simple, transparent, defensible.

**Weighted average**: compute each component's contribution, sum,
convert to letter only at end. For example: homework 30%, midterm
25%, final 35%, paper 10%. Each normalized to 100 then weighted.

**Decide letter thresholds in advance** and state in syllabus. Most
common: 90–100 A, 80–89 B, etc. Avoid moving thresholds
retroactively; that's how students stop trusting grading.

**Handle edge cases by policy, not mood**: students who fall 0.5%
below a threshold — will you round up, round down, use other
evidence? Decide in advance.

**Review the distribution** before final submission. An unusually
skewed distribution (e.g., everyone above 90, or a cluster at 89)
often signals a calibration issue worth checking.

## Preventing Academic Dishonesty

Most academic dishonesty is situational, not character-driven.
Students cheat when the incentive is high, the opportunity is
present, and the risk is low. Structure the course to lower the
first two and raise the third.

### Reducing incentive

- **De-emphasize grades in course culture**. When everything is
  framed around the grade, cheating is economically rational.
- **Ensure challenging-but-achievable standards**. Students don't
  typically cheat on work they can do; they cheat on work they feel
  they can't. Structure pacing, scaffolding, and early success to
  reduce desperation.
- **Provide genuine learning resources**: tutoring, office hours,
  study groups. Students who have legitimate help channels use
  illegitimate ones less.

### Reducing opportunity on exams

- **Change exam questions each term.** Keep an item bank, rotate.
- **Use alternate forms** (scrambled orders, different versions)
  for multiple-choice tests.
- **Seat students randomly in alternate chairs** if possible.
- **Clear calculator memories** before exams for quantitative
  courses.
- **Proctor actively**: move, walk the aisles, spend time in the
  back. Cheating requires checking the proctor's location; making
  the proctor's location unpredictable reduces opportunity.
- **Supply scratch paper** — don't let students use their own.
- **Collect exams systematically** — don't let students pile them
  at the front in chaos at the end.

### Reducing opportunity on writing

- **Require submission of drafts**, outlines, or topic proposals
  before the final paper. A student who submitted an outline and
  drafts can't easily submit a purchased paper.
- **Discuss paper topics in office hours** early in the process.
- **Assign topics that require current or local material** — recent
  events, community observations, specific course material — rather
  than evergreen topics that exist in paper mills.
- **Short in-class writing** throughout the term gives you a
  baseline for each student's voice.

### The disclosure conversation

State clearly, in the syllabus and in class, what you consider
dishonesty: what collaboration is permitted, what constitutes
plagiarism, what's fraudulent. Ambiguity is an invitation. "You may
discuss problems with classmates but must write up your own
solution independently" is clearer than "no cheating."

### Fraudulent excuses

Davis's survey: over two-thirds of college students admit to using
at least one fraudulent excuse. Distinguish:
- **Legitimate** excuses (genuine emergency, verified illness)
- **Fraudulent** (fabricated to avoid work)
- **Unacceptable** (truthful but not justification — "I forgot")

Policy tactics:
- State what counts as acceptable in the syllabus
- Require some form of proof for major absences
- **Structure the course so excuses matter less** — drop the lowest
  quiz, allow one late assignment without penalty, build in buffer.
  When the cost of one missed item is low, fraud is less tempting.

Davis notes: "my grandmother died" is more often true than not.
Cynicism that refuses all excuses is both unkind and inaccurate.

### If you suspect cheating

- **Document**: what you observed, when, and what you did.
- **Check your institution's policy** before confronting. Most
  institutions have defined procedures and confrontation protocols.
- **Preserve the evidence**: copies of papers, exam materials,
  behavior records.
- **Don't act in anger.** The conversation with the student should
  happen after cooling off, with clear documentation.

## What To Look For When Critiquing Course Grading

### Structure
- Are multiple grade components used, or one or two high-stakes?
- Are weights stated in syllabus?
- Is there a first graded assignment early enough to inform
  add/drop?

### Fairness
- Criterion-referenced or curve? (Default to criterion.)
- Are non-academic factors (attendance, attitude) folded in
  inappropriately?
- If multiple graders, is there a calibration process?
- Are grade disputes handled via clear procedure?

### Support for learning
- Does the grading structure encourage iteration (rewrites, drop
  lowest, etc.) or treat every assessment as terminal?
- Are students kept informed of standing throughout?

### Integrity
- Are dishonesty expectations clearly stated?
- Are major papers staged (drafts, outlines) or single-submission?
- Are exam security practices sensible?
- Is there sufficient flexibility (drop lowest, one free late) to
  reduce cheating incentive?

## Red Flags

- **Curve-graded course** in which student collaboration is
  discouraged structurally.
- **Single high-stakes assessment** (one exam = whole grade).
- **Grades include subjective "participation" or "attitude" at
  significant weight.**
- **No calibration among TAs** — wildly different standards across
  sections.
- **Grade thresholds moved retroactively** to curve the class down.
- **Academic honesty policy vague or absent** in syllabus.
- **All exams use identical questions reused across semesters**, and
  past exams circulate freely.
- **Papers submitted only at end**, with no intermediate stages.
- **Instructor treats fraud as a character issue** rather than as a
  signal about course structure (incentives, opportunities,
  scaffolding).

## Summary

**Grade learning, not behavior.** Non-academic factors corrupt the
grade's meaning as a learning indicator.

**Multiple, moderate-stakes components** beat one or two high-stakes
assessments. They provide formative information, reduce single-event
risk, and support actual learning.

**Transparency reduces disputes.** Clear criteria, visible rubrics,
stated weights, consistent thresholds — students may not like every
grade, but they can see where it came from.

**Most dishonesty is structural.** Reduce incentive (don't
over-weight grades, provide support), opportunity (exam security,
staged writing), and cost-of-failure (drop lowest, flex deadlines) —
and you'll have less to deal with than if you focus on detection
and punishment.

**Handle disputes as pedagogical events.** They're uncomfortable but
they're real information — about your grading, the student's
understanding, or both.
