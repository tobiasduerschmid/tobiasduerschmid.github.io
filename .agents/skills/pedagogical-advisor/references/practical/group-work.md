# Group Work and Collaborative Learning

ICAP (see `practices/icap-engagement.md`) establishes that
Interactive engagement — genuine co-construction among peers — is the
highest-leverage form of engagement. This file addresses the
practical question: how do you actually set up groups that achieve
that, rather than groups where one student does all the work, two
coast, and one checks email? The theory doesn't tell you how to form
groups, assign roles, grade fairly, or diagnose why a group is
failing. Davis's *Tools for Teaching* (chapters 18–19) does.

## Why Group Work Often Fails

Before prescribing tactics, note the common failure modes. If
students resist group work, it's often because their prior experience
has been bad — and that bad experience came from predictable
failures:

- **Free-riders.** One or two members carry the work; others collect
  the grade.
- **Unequal skill or confidence.** A strong student dominates; others
  defer.
- **No accountability for individual contribution.** The group grade
  masks wide variation in effort.
- **Vague task.** "Work together on this" with no structure
  degenerates into the strongest student writing while others watch.
- **Poor group composition.** Friend groups underperform
  task-assigned groups because social dynamics override task focus.
- **Insufficient time.** 10 minutes for a task that needs 25.

Every tactic below addresses one or more of these failure modes.

## Designing the Task

The task defines whether group work will be collaborative or
performative.

**A good group task has these properties**:
- **Genuinely requires multiple contributions.** If one person could
  do it in half the time, the group adds nothing. Good group tasks
  involve multiple perspectives, divided subtasks, or a conceptual
  problem where explaining to peers is the point.
- **Has a concrete, observable output.** A list, a diagram, a ranked
  set of alternatives, a solved problem, a prediction — something
  the group can point to and say "we produced this."
- **Has explicit constraints.** Time limit, output format, role
  distribution if relevant. "Discuss the reading" is not a task;
  "identify the three strongest objections to the author's
  argument and rank them" is.
- **Requires synthesis or decision**, not just information exchange.
  Students reporting what they already knew is a recitation, not
  collaboration.

**Bad task signals**:
- Could be done alone faster
- Has a single right answer easily lookup-able
- Allows division into parallel work with no integration
- Has no deliverable

## Forming Groups

**Don't let students self-select into friend groups** for substantive
work. Friend groups are comfortable but typically less productive:
the social contract of friendship conflicts with honest disagreement,
which is exactly what productive group work needs.

**Group sizes**:
- *Pairs*: low-stakes tasks, quick checks, think-pair-share. High
  participation but less diverse input.
- *Trios*: often the sweet spot. Enough perspectives without
  free-rider cover.
- *Four to five*: substantive projects over longer periods. Beyond
  five, free-riding becomes structural.
- *Six or more*: almost always subdivides into parallel subgroups.

**Composition strategies**:
- **Random assignment** (numbering off, drawing cards) — fair and
  fast
- **Heterogeneous skill distribution** — mix strong and weaker
  students deliberately; the strong learn by explaining, the weaker
  by being explained to (well-documented effect)
- **Complementary skills** — if the task needs writing, data
  analysis, and presentation, form groups with each represented
- **Diverse backgrounds or perspectives** — for tasks where diverse
  input is the point

**For long-term groups** (over weeks), Davis recommends a "group
contract" at the start: members agree on meeting times, deadlines,
communication norms, and how they'll handle conflicts. Surfacing
these up front prevents them from being discovered in crisis.

## Structuring Group Work

Structure turns "time to talk" into productive work.

**Assign roles when appropriate**:
- *Recorder* — captures the group's output
- *Reporter* — summarizes to the whole class
- *Facilitator* — keeps the group on task, manages time
- *Devil's advocate* — required to challenge consensus
- *Summarizer* — restates periodically

Rotate roles across meetings so no one is stuck as the scribe.

**Give explicit time budgets.** "Take 10 minutes to identify three
themes, then 5 minutes to rank them." Without timing, groups either
rush or drift.

**Circulate actively.** Move between groups. Listen briefly to each.
This does three things: monitors progress, catches groups that have
stalled, and signals that you'll notice both engagement and
free-riding.

**Don't answer too readily when groups call you over.** Often a
group calling for help wants you to resolve their disagreement so
they can stop thinking. Ask instead: "What have you considered?
What's the disagreement?" Make them continue the work.

**Build in reporting structure.** Groups produce higher-quality work
when they know they'll present to the class. Common formats:
- One group presents; others critique
- Round-robin, each group reports one point
- Gallery walk: groups post output on the wall; others circulate
  with feedback notes
- Jigsaw: each group becomes expert in one piece, then reforms
  into mixed groups to share

## Collaborative Learning Techniques

**Think-pair-share**: individual think (30–60 seconds), pair discuss
(1–2 minutes), whole-class share. Low-stakes, fits in any lecture,
scales to any class size.

**Jigsaw**: divide content into pieces; each group masters one;
reform into groups with one member from each piece; members teach
each other. Works best for content that legitimately divides (e.g.,
multiple case studies, distinct theoretical positions).

**Think-aloud pairs**: pair students; one solves a problem out loud
while the other listens and asks clarifying questions; swap. Forces
articulation and catches misconceptions.

**Structured debate**: divide class into positions; each group
develops arguments; debate with turn structure; conclude with
reflection on which arguments were strongest (regardless of original
position).

**Case studies**: present a scenario with ambiguity; groups analyze;
compare approaches. Works across disciplines — business, medicine,
engineering, humanities. Good cases have enough detail to analyze and
enough ambiguity to generate disagreement.

**Role plays**: students take designated positions or roles and
reason from within them. Particularly powerful for perspective-taking
(historical figures, stakeholder positions, opposing parties).
Requires enough structure that students aren't just improvising.

## Grading Group Work Fairly

This is where most group-work plans collapse. A shared grade for
genuinely different contributions is unfair to strong contributors
and rewards free-riders.

**Separate group and individual components.** Common structures:
- Group product = X% of grade; individual component (quiz on content,
  reflection paper, individual contribution) = Y%
- Group submits a product *and* a record of who did what; individual
  grades adjusted accordingly
- Peer evaluation contributes a percentage of the individual grade

**Use peer evaluation carefully.** Anonymous peer evaluations at
the end of a project let members report who contributed what. They
should be anonymous, based on specific behaviors (not general
impressions), and should count but not dominate. A common formula:
peer evaluation raises or lowers individual grade by up to 10–15% of
the group grade.

**Make individual accountability visible** during the process, not
just at grading. If members know they'll be rated, behavior changes
during the work, not after.

**Provide feedback at the group level** on group process, not just
product: "You handled disagreement productively" or "Several members
reported unequal distribution of work — address this before the next
phase."

## When Groups Break Down

Signs of group dysfunction and what to do:

- **One member dominates**: privately suggest they practice
  "facilitator" role; intervene in class to redirect airtime.
- **One member disengages**: often signals they're lost or that the
  group has made them feel unwelcome. Check in privately.
- **Group is stuck in friendly agreement with no productive
  disagreement**: push on assumptions, assign devil's advocate role,
  give them a more divisive question.
- **Two subgroups forming within a group**: divide the task so each
  subgroup has a defined piece that must integrate — forces cross-
  subgroup communication.
- **Conflict personalizes**: refocus on task and evidence; model
  the move from "you're wrong" to "I think the evidence points
  the other way."
- **Group-wide disengagement**: often the task is under- or
  over-specified. Either give a tighter prompt or open it up.

## What To Look For When Critiquing Group Work Plans

### Task design
- Does the task genuinely require multiple contributions?
- Is the output concrete and observable?
- Is timing explicit?

### Group formation
- Is composition deliberate or random (not friend-chosen)?
- Is size appropriate to task?
- For long-term groups, is there a process contract?

### Structure
- Are roles assigned if appropriate?
- Is there a plan for reporting or synthesis?
- Does the instructor plan to circulate?

### Accountability
- Is there individual accountability built in?
- Is there a peer-evaluation mechanism?
- Does the grading structure discourage free-riding?

## Red Flags

- **"Break into groups and discuss" with no specific task or
  output.**
- **Single group grade for multi-student project**, no peer
  evaluation, no individual component.
- **Self-selected groups for substantive work**, producing
  friend-group dynamics.
- **Groups larger than 5** for most tasks.
- **No circulation by the instructor** — groups work unsupervised,
  so neither support nor accountability happens.
- **Reporting plan is "each group will present"** with no structure
  or response from other groups — reporters talk, others check email.
- **Persistent free-rider complaints** that the instructor treats
  as student problems rather than structural ones.

## Summary

**Good group work requires good task design.** If the task could be
done alone faster, the group will not engage. Design for genuine
interdependence.

**Structure beats exhortation.** Telling students to "collaborate
well" doesn't work; giving them roles, time budgets, and reporting
requirements does.

**Individual accountability prevents free-riding.** Purely shared
grades reward coasting. Build in individual components and peer
evaluation.

**Circulate actively.** Your presence in the room during group work
is signal and support simultaneously — groups adjust when they know
you're listening.

**Don't assume students know how to work in groups.** Many don't, and
their prior experience has often been bad. Teach the process
explicitly — norms, roles, handling disagreement — just as you would
teach content.
