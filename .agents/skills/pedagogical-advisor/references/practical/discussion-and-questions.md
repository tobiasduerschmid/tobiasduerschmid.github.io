# Discussion Facilitation and Questioning

ICAP (see `practices/icap-engagement.md`) tells you why discussion
can work: learners who articulate, respond, and co-construct reach
deeper understanding than those who passively receive. This file
addresses the practical problem: *how do you actually run a
discussion that achieves that?* Unstructured discussions fail reliably
— a few students dominate, most stay silent, threads drift, and the
conceptual payoff is thin. The moves below, drawn from Davis's *Tools
for Teaching* (chapters 8–11), are what experienced discussion
leaders actually do.

## Before the Discussion

A "spontaneous" discussion that works is almost always carefully
prepared.

**Clarify the purpose.** Discussion is useful for: articulating
ideas in public, formulating and testing arguments, evaluating
evidence, hearing diverse perspectives, and thinking through
problems. It is *not* the right format for transmitting large
amounts of new factual content — lecture or reading does that more
efficiently. Use discussion when the goal genuinely requires student
thinking to be visible.

**Prepare the students.** Discussions are as good as the preparation
learners bring. Mechanisms for ensuring preparation:
- Distribute 4–6 study questions with each reading
- Require a one- or two-paragraph "reaction piece" before class
- Ask students to bring a provocative question with a sentence on
  why it's worth discussing
- Have them identify unfamiliar vocabulary, the author's thesis, and
  subtopics for which they could ask a question

**Prepare yourself.** Plan multiple possible openings (if one fails,
switch). Have 3–4 specific questions you could ask at different
points. Plan a rough timing: when will you shift activities? A
stimulating discussion requires both spontaneity and a backbone.

**Set norms at the start of the term.** On the first day or in the
syllabus, define the role discussion will play: participation is
expected, the classroom is a "safe place" to test ideas, preparation
matters. Davis quotes several norm-setting lists; the essentials:
- Critique ideas, not people
- Goal is best answer, not winning
- Listen; be willing to restate what others said before disagreeing
- Tolerate opposing views; change your mind when evidence warrants

## Opening a Discussion

Don't start with "so, what did you think of the reading?" That's an
open invitation for silence.

**Specific openers that work** (Davis names many):
- **Refer to study questions**: "Which of the questions did you
  find most difficult to answer — and why?"
- **Write-first, then-discuss**: "Take two minutes to write down
  your answer to this question. Then we'll share." This lets
  slower processors and quieter students think before the fast
  responders dominate.
- **Critical incident**: "Recall a time in your own life when…"
  then connect incidents to the reading.
- **Memorable images**: "What one image from the reading stayed
  with you?" List on the board; explore themes.
- **"Dumbest question" game**: frees students to ask without
  fear; treat as a signal of where understanding is genuinely
  thin.
- **Controversial prompt**: pose a question, have students
  physically group by position, each group identifies two supporting
  arguments.
- **"Truth statements"**: divide into small groups, each composes
  three true statements about a topic. Statements go on the board;
  whole class discusses.
- **Brainstorm**: open generation with no critique, 5 minutes; then
  evaluate as a group.
- **Think-pair-share**: same prompt, individual thinking, paired
  discussion, then whole class — scales down the risk of speaking.

**Have three or four openers in mind**, not just one. If the first
falls flat, switch.

## Guiding the Discussion

Once started, the instructor's job shifts from ignition to
stewardship.

**Make structure visible.** List the day's questions on the board so
the class can see where the discussion is heading. Give brief interim
summaries as the discussion moves through them.

**Listen for multiple things at once**:
- *Substance*: is the student's logic sound?
- *Tone*: how certain or uncertain are they?
- *Relation to the arc*: does this build on what came before, or
  redirect?
- *Mood of the room*: engaged, confused, checked out?
- *What's left unsaid*: whose voice is missing?

**Use nonverbal cues to manage flow.** A raised hand stops an
interrupter. Stepping back from a speaker lets them see the rest of
the room and address it. An expectant look at a quiet student invites
entry. Physical movement shifts energy without requiring words.

**Bring it back when it drifts**. "We seem to have lost sight of the
original point. Let's pick up the question about…" Or "Peter, you
have a good point — but does it apply directly to the issue of
censorship?" Be explicit about redirection; students can feel lost
otherwise.

**Handle disagreement productively but don't shut it down.** Some
conflict stimulates thought. Too much escalation kills it.
Defusers when things heat up:
- "Let's slow down a moment."
- "It seems we need to identify what we agree on and what we don't.
  Let's start with the agreements."
- "Those who want to continue this point can do so outside class;
  let's move on."

**But don't flatten productive disagreement**:
- "Who doesn't agree with what's being said?"
- "What would a devil's advocate say?"
- "Can someone present the opposing view?"

**Change the task if the discussion stagnates.** Expect one or two
lulls — students need time to think. But if attention is wandering,
switch: pair students for a short task, shift from theory to
application, go to the board, give a 30-second writing prompt. Don't
try to revive a dead discussion by asking louder variants of the same
question.

**Signs a discussion is breaking down**:
- Excessive nit-picking over tangential points
- Points repeated with no development
- Private side conversations
- Members taking sides and refusing to engage
- Ideas attacked before they're fully expressed
- Apathetic participation

Any of these is a signal to change the task, not to push harder.

## Asking Good Questions

The quality of the discussion is bounded by the quality of the
questions.

**Avoid questions with single correct answers** ("What is entropy?").
These reduce discussion to recitation. Prefer:
- "What about entropy stood out to you in the reading?"
- "Can you give an example of entropy from outside the text?"
- "Where does this concept break down?"

**Vary the cognitive demand**. Use Bloom's taxonomy (see
`design/bloom-taxonomy.md`) to plan a mix:
- *Remember/understand*: "What did the author claim about X?"
- *Apply*: "How would this framework apply to case Y?"
- *Analyze*: "What are the author's underlying assumptions?"
- *Evaluate*: "How strong is the evidence for this position?"
- *Create*: "How might you design a study to test this?"

Higher-order questions take longer to answer. Allow the silence.
Research consistently finds that instructors wait about 1 second
before rephrasing or answering their own question; waiting 5–10
seconds produces dramatically longer, more thoughtful student
responses.

**Use wait time deliberately**. This is one of the single most
powerful levers in discussion and it costs nothing.

## Fielding Student Questions

When students ask questions, how you respond shapes whether they ask
again.

**Take the question seriously, even if it seems off-base.** A
question that sounds confused often points to a real conceptual
knot — either in this student or in several. Treat it as signal,
not noise.

**Useful response moves**:
- **Echo and check**: "So you're asking whether X — is that
  right?" Often clarifies the question itself and signals you heard
  it.
- **Redirect to the class**: "Great question — who has thoughts?"
  This builds the norm that learning is distributed, not
  instructor-mediated.
- **"Hold that"**: "That's an excellent question and we'll come
  back to it in 10 minutes when we get to…" (Actually come back
  to it.)
- **Admit not knowing**: "I don't know — let's figure out where to
  find out." This models intellectual humility and is almost always
  well-received.

**Avoid**:
- Dismissive answers ("We'll cover that later" without returning)
- Over-elaborate answers that shut down follow-up
- Answering a slightly different question than was asked
- Visible impatience — even nonverbal — which teaches the whole
  class not to ask

**If the question reveals a misconception**, don't correct it alone.
Use it: "A lot of people think that. Let's work through why it's
actually more complicated…" Treat the misconception as shared,
not as the asker's individual fault.

## Encouraging Participation

Uneven participation is the most common failure of discussion.

**Use pair or small-group work as an on-ramp**. Students who won't
speak to 30 classmates will often speak to one. After a pair
discussion, calling on a pair to share reduces the vulnerability —
they're reporting a collaborative conclusion.

**Call on students deliberately but respectfully**. Cold-calling can
work if norms are set ("I may call on anyone; you may always say 'I'd
rather pass'") but becomes punitive otherwise. Davis describes one
instructor who lets students write their name on a paper to opt out
of being called on — most don't, and some change their minds.

**Notice who speaks and who doesn't**. A quick tally during a class
— small marks next to names — reveals patterns you'd miss otherwise.
If the same 4 students always speak and 20 never do, that's
actionable information.

**Phrase questions so more students can respond**. A narrow factual
question has one right answer; an interpretive question has many
reasonable ones. The latter lowers the stakes for speaking.

**Reinforce contributions explicitly** but don't praise trivially.
"Cherry's point about pollution really synthesized what we'd been
discussing" is more useful than "great point." Specific
acknowledgment teaches students what contributes to the conversation.

**Handle dominant students.** A few students over-speaking crowds
out others. Strategies:
- Use small-group structures more
- Assign specific roles ("today, I want people who haven't spoken
  much yet to answer first")
- Direct address: "We've heard a lot from Mark — let's hear from
  someone who hasn't yet spoken"

## Closing the Discussion

Closure is often skipped under time pressure, but it's where the
learning consolidates.

- **Summarize the main points** — or better, ask students to.
  "Who can summarize the key issues we've raised?" (Or assign a
  student at the start to be the day's summarizer.)
- **Name what's unresolved**. Honesty about loose ends is more
  educational than false tidiness.
- **Connect forward**: how does this set up the next class or
  reading?
- **Minute paper at the end**: "One thing you're taking away, one
  question you still have." Collect anonymously; use to open the
  next session.

## What To Look For When Critiquing a Discussion Plan or Class

### Preparation
- Were students given a way to prepare?
- Are there specific questions or tasks, or just "discuss the
  reading"?

### Structure
- Is there a clear opening that isn't "what did you think?"
- Are questions varied in cognitive level?
- Is there a plan for wait time?
- Is there a mechanism for participation beyond volunteering?

### Facilitation
- Does the instructor plan to use pairs or small groups as on-ramps?
- Are there defined norms about respectful disagreement?
- Is there a plan for managing dominant students and eliciting
  quiet ones?

### Closure
- Is there planned time for synthesis?
- Is there a mechanism for capturing unresolved questions?

## Red Flags

- **"Discussion class" where only 4–5 students speak and the
  instructor fills the silences.**
- **Every question is factual recall.**
- **Instructor waits less than 2 seconds before rephrasing or
  answering their own question.**
- **No structure — just "any thoughts?" for 50 minutes.**
- **Dominant students go unchecked**, and quiet students remain
  invisible all term.
- **Disagreement handled by shutting it down** (in either direction —
  squelching real conflict, or letting it escalate to personal).
- **No closing synthesis** — class ends when time runs out.

## Summary

**A good discussion is prepared, not improvised.** The spontaneity
you see comes from the plan you don't.

**Questions are the engine.** Avoid questions with single right
answers; vary cognitive level; wait after asking.

**Participation is a structural problem.** Uneven participation
almost always reflects missing structure, not unmotivated students.
Pair work, specific prompts, norms, and deliberate calling distribute
the air.

**Facilitation is stewardship, not performance.** Your job is to
start the conversation, keep it on track without squelching it, and
help the class see what was learned.
