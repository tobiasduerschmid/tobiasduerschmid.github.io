# Formative Feedback Mechanisms

End-of-semester course evaluations arrive too late to help the
students filling them out. They also typically elicit vague ratings
rather than actionable information. **Fast feedback** — Davis's term
for informal in-semester mechanisms — solves both problems: it tells
you in time to fix things, and it gathers specific information you
can act on.

This file complements `practices/feedback.md`, which addresses
feedback *to students* (on their work). This file addresses feedback
*from students* (on your teaching) — and feedback *to students on
their own understanding* that doubles as feedback to you. The
techniques here are drawn from Davis's *Tools for Teaching* chapter
41, with supplementary ideas from Angelo & Cross's *Classroom
Assessment Techniques*.

## Why Fast Feedback Matters

Two distinct things need monitoring during a course, and both are
invisible without deliberate structure:

1. **Are students understanding the material?** Students rarely
   volunteer "I didn't get that" mid-lecture. Silence is ambiguous —
   it could mean "clear," "confused," or "checked out." You can't
   tell without a mechanism.

2. **Is the course working for them?** Pace, workload, clarity,
   assignments — students have reliable intuitions about what's
   helping and what isn't, but they won't raise these issues unless
   asked.

The techniques below surface both kinds of information with minimal
class time.

## Classroom Assessment Techniques (CATs)

These are quick, low-stakes, usually anonymous techniques that take
2–5 minutes of class. They reveal student understanding in real time.

### The One-Minute Paper

Probably the most-used CAT. At the end of class, ask students to
write briefly on two questions:
1. What is the most important thing you learned today?
2. What question is uppermost in your mind at the end of class?

Collect anonymously. Read them before the next class. The aggregate
response tells you:
- Whether the intended main point *landed* as the main point
- Which concepts students are genuinely confused about
- What questions they would never raise in class

Time cost: ~2 minutes of class + ~10 minutes of your reading.
Information gained: often the single best per-minute investment
in your teaching.

### The Muddiest Point

Variant, from a Harvard statistics professor Davis cites: ask
students to name the "muddiest point" in today's lecture. Sometimes
paired with:
- What percent of the mud was due to unclear presentation?
- Lack of opportunity to ask questions?
- Your own lack of preparation?
- Other?

The causal attribution is useful — it separates "I didn't study
enough" from "the instructor skipped a step."

### Key-Concept List

At the end of a unit: "Write 3–5 key concepts or main ideas from
this unit." Reveals whether students identify the same core ideas
you intended to emphasize. Mismatch between your list and theirs is
diagnostic — either the emphasis didn't come through, or the
emphasis was on the wrong things.

### Define, Associate, Apply

"As I understand it, the main idea of today's session was…"; "A good
example of an application is…"; "This idea connects to the following
concepts, people, or events…" Reveals whether students can
articulate, exemplify, and connect the idea — not just recognize it.

### Background Knowledge Probe

Given at the start of a new topic: "What do you already know about
X?" Five-minute exercise, often surprising. Reveals the prior
knowledge you're actually building on, which is often not what the
syllabus assumes.

### Chain Notes

Pass a notebook or sheet around during class. As each student
receives it, they briefly note "the main point being discussed right
now" and pass it along. At the end, you have a time-stamped record of
what students perceived the topic to be at each moment. Useful for
catching drift — where did class get off track?

### Document the Misconception

After teaching something you know is typically confusing: "Describe,
in your own words, what X means and give one example." Mark anonymous
responses as correct / partially correct / wrong. The partial and
wrong ones are more informative than the correct ones — they show
exactly which part of the concept is failing to land.

## Structural Mechanisms

Beyond per-class CATs, there are course-level structures for getting
feedback.

### Mid-Semester Feedback Form

Around week 6–7 of a 14-week course: distribute a short anonymous
questionnaire. Ideally 4–6 questions. Focus on things you could
actually change this term:
- What's helping your learning?
- What's hindering it?
- Is the pace right?
- Is the workload reasonable?
- What one thing would improve the course?

**Leave the room** while students complete it. Ask a student
volunteer to collect and deliver. Responses will be franker without
your presence.

### Small-Group Instructional Diagnosis (SGID)

A variant, often facilitated by a colleague or teaching center:
instructor leaves class for last 15 minutes; a facilitator asks
students in small groups to agree on:
- Something that's helping learning
- Something that's hindering it
- Suggestions for improvement

Groups report; facilitator summarizes; reports back to instructor.
The group process surfaces consensus (not just outlier complaints)
and the facilitation removes social pressure on individual students.

### Student Liaison Committee

2–4 student representatives who meet with you periodically. They
gather concerns informally from classmates. Works well in larger
classes where you can't get to everyone. Rotate membership across
the term so no small group monopolizes the channel.

### Management Team

More involved: a team of students meets regularly, with you joining
periodically, to identify issues and recommend improvements. More
effort but produces deeper feedback on course organization, readings,
pace, and structure. Works best when you treat the team as genuine
collaborators, not focus-group subjects.

### Email, Office Hour, or Suggestion Box Channels

Standing channels for asynchronous feedback. Key point: if you set
them up, you must actually monitor and respond. An ignored channel is
worse than none.

## Responding to Feedback

Getting feedback is half the work. Responding visibly is the other
half.

**Respond at the next class.** Ideally the very next session.
Students need to see that their input matters.

**Sort feedback into three categories**:
- *Things you can change this semester* (pace, assignment turnaround,
  clarifying material)
- *Things you'll change next time* (textbook, syllabus structure)
- *Things you won't change, and why* (number of quizzes, grading
  scheme)

Be transparent about all three. If you won't change something,
explain the pedagogical reason — "I'm keeping weekly quizzes because
research on retrieval practice shows it strongly supports retention"
— rather than dismissing.

**Don't be defensive.** Even sharply worded feedback usually has a
real issue underneath. Lead with the kernel of truth before
contextualizing.

**Acknowledge specifically.** "Several of you noted that the
problem-set deadlines are bunching up — I'm moving PS3 by one week"
tells students their feedback was read and acted on. Generic "thanks
for the feedback" doesn't.

**Read the positive comments first.** Negative feedback has
disproportionate salience. Balance is restored by reading positive
comments before you draft responses.

## What To Look For When Critiquing Course Design

- Is there a mechanism for in-semester feedback on understanding? (A
  course with no CATs is flying blind about comprehension.)
- Is there a mid-semester feedback point built into the schedule?
- If there are structural channels (liaison committee, management
  team), are they actually being used?
- Does the instructor have a plan for *responding* to feedback, not
  just collecting it?

## Red Flags

- **"Any questions?" is the only comprehension check used all term.**
- **No mid-semester feedback** — only end-of-term evaluations, which
  arrive too late.
- **Feedback is solicited but never visibly responded to.** Students
  stop giving feedback when they sense it disappears into a void.
- **Feedback channels that target the end of the term** only (when
  changes cannot be made).
- **Defensive responses to feedback** — dismissing student concerns
  as misunderstandings rather than signals.
- **Minute papers used for attendance-taking rather than formative
  signal** — instructor collects but doesn't read them.

## Summary

**End-of-term evaluations are not enough.** They arrive too late and
elicit too-vague information.

**Fast feedback is cheap.** Two minutes of class time for a minute
paper, 10 minutes of your reading, and you have actionable
information about comprehension and course quality.

**The best CATs serve double duty.** The minute paper tells you
what students learned *and* prompts retrieval practice *and* gives
them a moment to consolidate. The muddiest-point tells you where to
re-teach *and* makes students articulate their confusion (which is
itself a learning event).

**Respond visibly.** Students who see their feedback acted on will
keep giving you useful feedback. Students who don't will stop — or
save it for the end-of-term evaluation where it arrives as
complaint rather than signal.
