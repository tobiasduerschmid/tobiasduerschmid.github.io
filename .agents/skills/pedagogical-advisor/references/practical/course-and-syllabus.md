# Course Planning, Syllabus, and First Day

The theory files cover *how* to design a course (backward design,
learning objectives, alignment). This file covers the practical craft:
what actually goes into a syllabus, how to construct it, how to set up
the course before students arrive, and how to run the first day so the
rest of the term has a fighting chance.

This material draws from Barbara Gross Davis's *Tools for Teaching*,
which distills classroom practice from hundreds of faculty into
concrete tactics. It's a tactical complement to the theoretical
frameworks in `design/backward-design.md` and `design/learning-objectives.md`.

## Before the Term: Course Planning

### Start from goals, then select content ruthlessly

The most common course-design failure is "coverage thinking" — trying
to teach everything in the field. Davis's summary: emphasize the core
concepts, stress enduring issues, and cut to the chase. In
mathematics, the thousands of equations reduce to a handful of basic
ideas; the instructor's job is to show students the underlying
structure rather than drill the surface variety.

Give students a **conceptual framework** — a theory, theme, typology,
or organizing controversy — on which to hang factual detail. Without
one, the field looks to novices like "an unruly mass of facts devoid
of logic or unifying principles." Refer back to the framework
repeatedly across the term.

### Structure the content deliberately

Material can be arranged chronologically, topically, concrete to
abstract, theory to application, or by increasing complexity. Davis
(citing Bergquist & Phillips) names three useful contrasts:
- **Micro/macro** — start with a detailed case, or with the broad
  context
- **Distal/proximal** — start with origins and background, or with a
  pressing current problem
- **Phenomenon/structure** — emphasize specific unique cases, or
  general patterns that cut across them

Pick one deliberately; don't let it emerge by accident.

### Sketch the term schedule early

List all class meetings. Mark holidays and breaks. Fill in tentative
topics and exam dates. A few practical moves:
- Leave open at least part of the class before each exam for
  catch-up or review.
- Leave extra time for topics you know are conceptually hard.
- Schedule **mid-semester feedback** (see `formative-feedback.md`)
  near the middle of the term.
- Give special attention to the first day and the last two or three
  classes (for integration and closure).

The planning question is not "what am I going to do each class?"
but "what are students going to do each class?" This flip is small
and important.

### Textbooks and readings

Choose texts that align with your goals. Per Davis (citing
McKeachie), **the textbook exerts more influence on what students
learn than the teaching method does.** Choose accordingly.

Explain to students *why* each reading was assigned — to reinforce
lectures, extend them, introduce a contrasting view, or provide
background. Don't leave this implicit. In the syllabus, link each
reading to the course objective it supports.

Workload calibration: two to three hours outside class per hour in
class is the traditional expectation at most universities. About 20
pages per hour is a reasonable estimate for moderately difficult
text; adjust for your students and material. Budget explicitly.

## Writing the Syllabus

The syllabus is a **learning tool**, not just an administrative
document. Err toward more detail rather than less. Davis's experienced
faculty report that detailed syllabi reduce student anxiety and
increase effectiveness.

A good syllabus includes (Davis, Altman & Cashin, Birdsall):

**Basic information**
- Course title, number, units, term, meeting time/place
- Instructor name, office, phone/email, office hours
- TA names and contact info if applicable
- Prerequisites (realistic, with pointers to refresh)

**Course purpose and structure**
- Overview of the course — what it's about, why students might care
- **3–5 learning objectives** — what students will know or be able
  to do at the end
- Conceptual structure — why topics are ordered as they are
- Format and activities — lecture, discussion, fieldwork, etc.

**Materials and assignments**
- Specific textbook(s) with edition and rationale
- Readings with sources and dates, ideally linked to objectives
- All assignments, papers, exams — with dates, format, expectations
- How each relates to the learning objectives

**Grading and policies**
- How grades are calculated (components and weights)
- Absolute scale vs. curve, extra credit policy
- Late work policy (fixed deduction, grace period, drop lowest, etc.)
- Attendance expectations
- Academic honesty expectations (see `grading-and-integrity.md`)
- Accommodations note: "Students with disabilities, please contact
  me during office hours to arrange accommodations"

**Course calendar**
- Sequence of topics, readings (with page numbers, not just
  chapters — helps students budget time), and due dates
- Exam dates fixed; topic dates may be tentative
- Important drop/withdraw deadlines

**Supplementary resources**
- Study hints and strategies
- Glossary of technical terms
- Past exams so students see the target at the start
- Campus tutoring and academic support resources
- Space for two or three classmates' contact info (for notes,
  study groups)

Davis's useful frame: the syllabus answers the student's real
questions — *what will I learn, what's the work, how am I graded, who
do I talk to, where do I go for help?* If the syllabus doesn't answer
these clearly, students will ask them all term.

## The First Day of Class

The first day sets the tone for the whole term. Davis identifies
three tasks: (1) handling administrative matters, (2) creating an
open, friendly classroom environment, and (3) setting course
expectations. Aim to do all three.

### Before class

- **Visit the room**. Find the lights, blinds, ventilation. Test any
  A/V equipment. See how well your voice carries to the back row.
  Write on the board and check legibility from the back.
- **Expect some awkwardness.** All teachers, especially new ones,
  feel apprehension before the first day. Students typically read
  nervous energy as enthusiasm.

### Administrative tasks

- Put the course name and number on the board (so anyone in the wrong
  room leaves before you start).
- Call roll; take attendance; explain add/drop policy.
- Review prerequisites; let students know if help is available.
- Hand out and walk through the syllabus.
- Review emergency and safety procedures.
- Mention academic honesty expectations (brief; detailed discussion
  later).

### Create a positive environment

- **Introduce yourself.** Not just your name — say something about
  how you came to the field, why it matters to you, what you find
  interesting. Students rate instructor enthusiasm as one of the
  strongest motivators (Sass, 1989).
- **Start learning student names.** Strategies: introduction cards,
  seating charts, name cards, roll call with pronunciation checks,
  photographs in smaller classes. This is not a nicety — students
  work harder for teachers who treat them as individuals rather
  than anonymous faces.
- **Give students a chance to meet each other.** Small-group
  introductions, "people search" activities, pairs interviewing
  each other. Early peer connections support participation all term.

### Set expectations and do real work

- Discuss course objectives — and ask students what *they* want to
  learn. Use their answers to identify interests and anticipated
  problem areas.
- Describe how class time will be spent and how discussion will
  work.
- Give study strategies — especially in introductory classes,
  where students often don't know how to prepare.
- **Plunge into substantive material.** Once administration is
  done, do real work — a demonstration, a core idea, a problem.
  This signals that class time is for learning, not
  paperwork-and-dismiss.
- Give an ungraded assignment for next class. (Ungraded because
  students may add/drop in the first week.)
- Consider ending with a **one-minute paper**: "What went well
  today? What questions do you have about the course?" Collect
  anonymously and open the next session with them.

## What To Look For When Critiquing

When reviewing a syllabus or course plan:

**Alignment**
- Are learning objectives stated? Are there 3–5, not 15 or 0?
- Do assignments and exams clearly assess the stated objectives?
- Is the reading load calibrated to the objectives, or just to
  "coverage"?

**Transparency**
- Is the grading breakdown explicit? Are weights given?
- Are late-work, extra-credit, and attendance policies stated?
- Is there an accommodations invitation?

**Student-centered framing**
- Does the syllabus speak *to* students (their questions, their
  time, their success) or *at* them (coverage, policies, warnings)?
- Is the rationale for readings and activities explicit?
- Is workload calibrated to a reasonable hours/week estimate?

**First-day plan**
- Is there more than administration in the plan?
- Is there a real activity — discussion, problem, demonstration?
- Is there a mechanism for students to surface concerns on day one?

## Red Flags

- **No learning objectives stated** — or objectives that are vague
  coverage statements ("students will learn about X").
- **Grading described only as percentages with no explanation of
  what the components measure.**
- **No mid-semester feedback built into the calendar.**
- **Syllabus longer than the assigned readings but containing only
  policies and warnings** — it reads as legal defense rather than
  orientation.
- **First-day plan is entirely syllabus review and dismissal.**
  Sets the tone that class time is for administration, not learning.
- **Workload not calibrated** — students have no way to budget
  time, and you'll hear about it for 14 weeks.
- **Readings listed without rationale.** Students don't know what
  they're reading for, so they treat it all equally — which usually
  means ignoring most of it.

## Summary

A course plan and syllabus are not administrative paperwork — they
are the first teaching artifacts the student encounters. Get them
right.

**Plan backward from objectives**, not forward from content. Name
the core ideas and cut ruthlessly around them.

**The syllabus is a learning tool.** It should answer the questions
students actually have, link each reading and assignment to a
purpose, and establish expectations in writing before the course
starts.

**The first day is not a throwaway.** Use it to set tone, establish
relationships, communicate expectations, *and* do real work.
