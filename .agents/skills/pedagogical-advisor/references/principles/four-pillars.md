# The Four Pillars of Learning

Source: Dehaene (2020), *How We Learn: Why Brains Learn Better Than Any Machine
… For Now*. Viking. Synthesizing cognitive neuroscience findings on human
learning.

Dehaene argues that human learning success rests on four cognitive-neural
systems that evolution equipped us with. When all four are engaged, learning
happens efficiently. When any is missing or weak, learning falters. The four
are simple to state and surprisingly powerful as a design heuristic.

## The Four Pillars

### 1. Attention

Attention is the set of mechanisms that *select* what information gets deep
processing. Working memory and learning are saturation-bound: billions of
bits flow in, only some get amplified and encoded. What gets attended to is
what gets learned.

**What the research shows:**
- Inattentive processing produces essentially no learning of the unattended
  material. Seitz et al., Watanabe et al., and others have shown that
  perceptual learning in the absence of attention is minimal.
- Attention has multiple components: vigilance (staying alert), selection
  (picking what to focus on), and orientation (shifting focus). Instruction
  engages all three.
- Attention is a limited resource. Splitting it across too many things
  produces none-of-them-well processing.

**Design implications:**
- **Direct attention explicitly.** Don't assume the learner will notice what
  matters. Say "notice that…", highlight it visually, structure it prosodically.
  This is Mayer's signaling principle in the vocabulary of multimedia learning.
- **Reduce competition for attention.** Extraneous graphics, redundant text,
  sidebars, interruptions — each splits the attention budget.
- **Respect the rhythm.** Sustained attention has limits. Structure sessions
  with changes of mode (input, discussion, practice) roughly every 10-15
  minutes in a live session, 5-7 minutes in video.
- **Make salience match importance.** If the most important thing on the
  slide is in 12pt at the bottom and a decorative image is at the top
  half, the learner's attention goes to the wrong thing.

**Red flags when critiquing:**
- Slides where you can't quickly identify what the learner is supposed to
  focus on.
- Long stretches of undifferentiated material with no signaling.
- Materials that require the learner to integrate information from multiple
  attention-competing sources.

### 2. Active engagement

Passive exposure produces weak learning. The brain learns when it is
*generating predictions, testing hypotheses, being surprised, being curious*.
A receptive-but-passive learner is a weak learner.

**What the research shows:**
- Curiosity has measurable neural correlates; curious learners encode more.
- Over-demonstration can suppress curiosity — studies show children given a
  toy with demonstration explore fewer features than those given the same
  toy without demonstration. The teacher's signal of "here's everything"
  closes off inquiry.
- Learning improves when learners generate, predict, and explain, rather than
  receive.
- This doesn't mean discovery-based learning without guidance; it means
  guided instruction that keeps the learner actively generating throughout.
  Pure discovery without guidance produces worse outcomes.

**Design implications:**
- **Ask for prediction before revelation.** "What do you think will happen?"
  before showing the demonstration. This primes encoding.
- **Ask for generation.** "Come up with an example of X" before providing
  examples. "Explain in your own words why…" before giving the official
  explanation.
- **Preserve productive uncertainty.** Don't tell learners that you've given
  them everything. Leave visible open questions, unknowns, frontiers.
- **Engagement ≠ activity for activity's sake.** The point is cognitive
  engagement — the learner thinking hard — not physical busyness. A learner
  sitting still and generating an explanation is more engaged than one
  "actively" filling in a worksheet mechanically.

See `practices/icap-engagement.md` for the ICAP framework's more detailed
taxonomy of engagement modes.

**Red flags when critiquing:**
- Materials where the learner's role is purely receptive across long
  stretches.
- "Active learning" that's actually active-in-the-ICAP-sense (following
  instructions) rather than constructive (generating).
- Over-demonstrated tutorials where every step is done for the learner.

### 3. Error feedback

The brain learns by *comparing predictions to reality* and correcting. Errors
are not failures; they are the very signal that drives learning. A learner
who never errs is either being under-challenged (nothing to correct) or
being shielded from feedback about their errors.

**What the research shows:**
- Neural error signals (prediction errors) are a core mechanism of plasticity.
  They tell the brain what needs to change.
- Error feedback is most effective when it's specific, timely, and — critically
  — given in a context where errors are not punished as failures.
- Punishing errors suppresses error signal reporting. Learners stop trying
  hard things, stop reporting confusion, and stop engaging.
- Practice testing (retrieval practice) is among the most effective study
  techniques — and part of why is that it generates error signals that
  studying alone does not.

**Design implications:**
- **Design tasks that produce errors.** Tasks so easy that everyone is
  correct all the time are under-challenging. Desirable difficulty (the
  Bjorks) is partly about ensuring enough errors to drive learning.
- **Give error feedback that specifies the gap.** Not just "wrong" but
  "here's what the answer was and why it's that instead."
- **Normalize errors.** "This is the kind of problem where most people get
  it wrong the first time. Let's see why." Growth-mindset climate.
- **Separate errors from punishment.** Formative practice should be
  low-stakes. High-stakes errors should be rare; by the time learners are
  being judged, they should have had many chances to make and correct errors.
- **Error-based learning sequences.** Ask a question, get a prediction,
  reveal the answer. The gap is where learning happens.

See `practices/feedback.md` for the Hattie & Timperley feedback framework.

**Red flags when critiquing:**
- Materials where the learner never makes or confronts errors until a high-
  stakes assessment.
- Climates where errors are framed as failures.
- Feedback mechanisms that only tell the learner "wrong" without helping
  close the gap.

### 4. Consolidation

Learning isn't finished when the session ends. The brain consolidates —
converts fragile working-memory states into durable long-term representations
— over time, especially during sleep and especially with spaced revisiting.

**What the research shows:**
- Consolidation has a clear neural signature, involving the hippocampus
  "training" the cortex, often during sleep. McClelland's complementary
  learning systems model predicts this structure and it's been broadly
  confirmed.
- Spacing effects are among the most robust findings in cognitive science.
  Material revisited across days/weeks is retained far better than material
  studied in a single block, even with equal total study time.
- Sleep matters. Consolidation during sleep improves retention; sleep
  deprivation impairs it.
- Practice that forces retrieval is particularly effective at driving
  consolidation, more so than additional study.

**Design implications:**
- **Build in spacing across a course.** Don't cover a topic in week 3 and
  never return to it. Interleave review, build cumulative assessments,
  design spiral curricula.
- **Use retrieval practice.** Low-stakes quizzes, self-check questions,
  "explain from memory" prompts. See `practices/effective-techniques.md`.
- **Respect learner sleep.** Workshop formats and intensives that run late
  evenings before morning continuation undercut consolidation.
- **Summarize and synthesize.** End-of-session synthesis (ideally generated
  by learners, not instructor) primes consolidation.
- **Follow up.** A 1-week, 1-month retrieval prompt after a workshop or
  module dramatically improves retention.

**Red flags when critiquing:**
- Materials that present and never revisit.
- Assessments that only cover the most recent content.
- "Study guides" that are pure summary with no retrieval, effectively
  designed for re-reading.
- Professional development or training that's one-and-done with no spaced
  follow-up.

## How the pillars work together

Dehaene's claim is that all four are necessary. A teacher can get attention
but fail at engagement (lectures where students are awake but passive). A
course can have engagement but fail at error feedback (discovery-based
approaches without enough guidance to catch wrong hypotheses). Practice can
have error feedback but fail at consolidation (blocked practice that works
in the moment, evaporates by next week).

When evaluating a piece of instructional material, a quick four-pillar
scan is useful:

- **Attention**: What is the learner focused on? Is it the right thing?
  Is it being cued well?
- **Active engagement**: What is the learner *doing* cognitively? Generating,
  predicting, explaining — or just receiving?
- **Error feedback**: Does the learner encounter errors they can learn from,
  with feedback that helps close the gap?
- **Consolidation**: Is there a plan for durable retention — spacing,
  retrieval, revisiting?

If any of these four is absent or weak, that's probably the highest-leverage
place to intervene.

## The synthesis statement for quick recall

> Attention amplifies what matters. Active engagement pushes the brain to
> predict. Error feedback corrects predictions that failed. Consolidation
> makes the corrections durable. Teaching that orchestrates all four lets
> the brain's native learning algorithm do its work. Teaching that neglects
> any of them fights against how the brain learns.
