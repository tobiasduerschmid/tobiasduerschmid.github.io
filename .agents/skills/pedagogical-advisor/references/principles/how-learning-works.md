# How Learning Works: The Seven Principles

Source: Ambrose, Bridges, DiPietro, Lovett, & Norman (2010), *How Learning
Works: Seven Research-Based Principles for Smart Teaching*. Jossey-Bass.

The seven principles below are domain-independent, experience-independent, and
cross-culturally relevant (though culture affects application). They cover the
intellectual, motivational, and developmental dimensions of learning, drawing
on cognitive, developmental, and social psychology, anthropology, education,
and diversity studies. They apply from K–12 through graduate school.

When critiquing or designing any educational content, these seven are a
useful checklist. Most pedagogical failures can be traced to violating one
or more of them.

## Principle 1: Students' prior knowledge can help or hinder learning

Learners don't enter a course as blank slates. Their prior knowledge — what
they know, what they think they know, what they've heard, what they've
half-remembered — acts as the foundation (or obstacle) for everything they
encounter next.

**What the research says:**
- New learning is more effective when it connects to existing knowledge.
  Information that "sticks" is information that finds something to stick to.
- Prior knowledge can be *insufficient* (they don't have the scaffolding you
  assume), *inaccurate* (they have misconceptions that actively contradict
  what you're teaching), or *inappropriate* (correct in another context but
  wrong here — e.g., the Newtonian intuitions students bring to relativity).
- Inaccurate prior knowledge is the most pernicious. Students will sometimes
  hear what you say, translate it into what their misconception would predict,
  and walk away with the misconception reinforced.

**What to do:**
- **Assess prior knowledge**, don't assume it. Pre-tests, concept inventories,
  informal questions at the start of a unit. For critical prerequisites, have
  explicit diagnostic checks.
- **Make prior knowledge explicit to the learner.** "You already know X;
  today we're going to extend that to Y." This is what chunking in
  working memory looks like in instructional design.
- **Surface misconceptions and address them directly.** Don't just present
  the correct version and hope the misconception fades. Research is clear
  that this often doesn't work — the correct version has to be held *against*
  the misconception, and the misconception shown to fail.
- **Use contrasting cases** to make the distinction between the misconception
  and the correct understanding visible.
- **Activate relevant prior knowledge** before introducing new material.
  Analogies, warm-up exercises, "what do you already know about…" prompts.

**What to look for when critiquing:**
- Does the material check or activate prior knowledge?
- Does it engage with likely misconceptions, or does it just present the
  "correct" version?
- Are there hidden prerequisites that aren't stated?
- Where there's a counterintuitive point, is the counterintuition made
  explicit?

## Principle 2: How students organize knowledge influences how they learn and apply what they know

Knowledge doesn't just accumulate; it organizes. Experts have deep, richly
connected knowledge structures; novices have sparse, surface-level ones. The
organization predicts what learners can do with the knowledge.

**What the research says:**
- Experts and novices organize knowledge of the same domain very differently.
  Experts organize around deep structure (principles, relationships, causes);
  novices organize around surface features (keywords, superficial similarities).
- The way knowledge is organized determines transfer. Learners who've organized
  around surface features can't apply knowledge to a novel context because the
  deep structure isn't visible to them.
- Instruction shapes organization, for better or worse. Textbooks that are
  encyclopedic rather than hierarchical leave learners with an encyclopedic
  rather than hierarchical mental model.

**What to do:**
- **Expose the expert structure.** Instead of letting learners infer the
  organization of a domain, show them explicitly. Concept maps, hierarchies,
  "here's how a physicist thinks about this problem."
- **Highlight deep features over surface features.** When working through
  examples, explicitly call attention to the principle being applied, not
  only the surface details.
- **Use multiple examples that vary on surface features but share deep
  structure.** This builds the abstraction. (This is Variation Theory applied.)
- **Ask learners to articulate structure.** Concept maps they build. Self-
  explanations of how topics relate. "How would you group these problems?"

**What to look for when critiquing:**
- Does the material just present information, or does it also show
  relationships between pieces of information?
- Are there moments where the organization is made explicit to the learner?
- Does the sequence of topics reflect a coherent mental model, or is it
  historical, alphabetical, or just list-like?

## Principle 3: Students' motivation determines, directs, and sustains what they do to learn

Learning takes effort; effort requires motivation. Motivation is shaped by
what the learner values about the outcome, how likely they think success is,
and the climate/context around the task.

**What the research says:**
- Two big levers: **value** (does the learner care about the outcome?) and
  **expectancy** (does the learner believe they can succeed with effort?).
  Both have to be above some threshold for sustained motivation.
- Value has multiple sources: attainment value (does it matter to who I am?),
  intrinsic value (is it inherently interesting?), instrumental/utility value
  (does it help me achieve something I care about?).
- Expectancy depends on prior successes, perceived difficulty, and the
  climate's messages about who succeeds here.
- The environment (climate) mediates all of this. A supportive climate can
  sustain motivation through hard material; a hostile or uninspiring one
  erodes it.

**What to do:**
- **Make utility value visible.** Connect the material to what the learner
  cares about, is doing, or will do. This is especially powerful for
  first-generation students and students who don't see themselves in the
  field.
- **Calibrate difficulty to support efficacy.** Too easy = boring = no value.
  Too hard = failed = no efficacy. Aim for the zone where success is earned
  through effort but is achievable.
- **Give learners some autonomy** — choice of topic, problem, approach. This
  is especially effective in longer assignments.
- **Build early successes**, especially for novices and anxious learners,
  before escalating to harder material.
- **Attend to climate**: the messages the course sends about who belongs,
  who succeeds, and what's valued.

**What to look for when critiquing:**
- Is the *why* visible? Or is the material presented as "here's what you
  need to know"?
- Is the calibration of difficulty going to sustain motivation or erode it?
- Is there any learner agency?
- What messages does the framing send about who can do this material?

## Principle 4: To develop mastery, students must acquire component skills, practice integrating them, and know when to apply what they have learned

Mastery isn't one big thing; it's many component skills that have to be
acquired, integrated, and *called forth at the right moment*. Each of these
is a separate design problem.

**What the research says:**
- Complex skills decompose into component skills that have to be practiced
  individually before they can be usefully combined. Experts have automated
  many components, freeing cognitive resources for the integrative work.
- Component practice without integration produces learners who have the parts
  but can't combine them. Integration practice without component mastery
  produces learners who flounder in the integration.
- *Knowing when to apply* a skill is a separate learning problem from *knowing
  how*. Learners who can solve physics problems when told "this is a momentum
  problem" often fail to recognize momentum problems in the wild.
- The **expert blind spot** means instructors, who have long since automated
  the components, often teach as if integration can be done from scratch.

**What to do:**
- **Decompose target skills** into components. Teach the components
  deliberately before expecting integrated performance.
- **Scaffold the integration**. First worked examples of the full skill, then
  partially worked, then independent. This is the worked-example effect in
  action.
- **Practice recognition of context** — when does this skill apply? Mixed
  practice that forces the learner to identify the problem type before
  applying a technique builds this.
- **Beware the expert blind spot**. Test your materials on a target-level
  learner. Their stumbles are informative.

**What to look for when critiquing:**
- Are component skills identified and taught separately before being
  integrated?
- Is there scaffolded practice of the integrated skill?
- Do exercises require the learner to recognize which skill applies, or are
  they pre-cued by chapter/section?
- Where's the expert blind spot — what's assumed that shouldn't be?

## Principle 5: Goal-directed practice coupled with targeted feedback enhances the quality of students' learning

This principle is essentially the entire feedback literature in one sentence.
The practice has to be aimed at something specific, challenging at the right
level, happening in sufficient quantity, and paired with feedback that
actually helps the learner close the gap between current and desired
performance.

**What the research says:**
- Generic practice doesn't produce the same gains as goal-directed practice.
  Doing lots of math problems is less effective than doing math problems
  aimed at a specific skill or misconception.
- Feedback is among the highest-leverage instructional interventions
  (Hattie's meta-analyses place it consistently near the top) — *when it's
  done well*. Done poorly, it can have zero or even negative effects.
- Feedback is more effective when it's about the *task* or the *process* of
  doing the task, less effective when it's about the *self* ("you're smart,"
  "you're struggling"). Praise-focused feedback is particularly weak.
- Feedback works best when timely (close to the performance), specific
  (pointing at the exact gap), and actionable (telling the learner what to
  change or try).

**What to do:**
- **Set specific, challenging goals** for each practice activity. Not just
  "practice writing" but "practice writing topic sentences that preview a
  paragraph's argument."
- **Give targeted feedback** at three levels: task (was it correct?),
  process (was the approach sound?), and self-regulation (how should the
  learner monitor themselves going forward?).
- **Make feedback actionable** by being specific about both the issue and
  the next step.
- **Provide feedback in time for the learner to act on it.** Feedback on
  a final exam is too late; feedback on a draft is actionable.

See `practices/feedback.md` for the full Hattie & Timperley framework.

**What to look for when critiquing:**
- Is the practice aimed at specific skills, or is it generic?
- What kind of feedback does the learner get, and when?
- Is the feedback actionable? Can the learner do something different next
  time based on it?

## Principle 6: Students' current level of development interacts with the social, emotional, and intellectual climate of the course to impact learning

Learners aren't just cognitive processors. They're developing people, with
social and emotional lives that intersect with their learning. The climate
of a course — the tone, the relationships, the messages — affects what's
possible cognitively.

**What the research says:**
- Climate has measurable effects on learning, persistence, and outcomes, not
  just feelings.
- Negative climates (hostile, dismissive, exclusionary) reduce performance
  below what the same students achieve in supportive climates — this is
  not a "soft" effect, it's a cognitive-load effect. Managing social threat
  costs working memory.
- Stereotype threat is real and measurable. Students in groups culturally
  stereotyped as worse at a subject underperform when that stereotype is
  salient.
- Students' social-emotional development varies — early undergraduates are
  often still developing comfort with ambiguity, peer interaction, and
  productive failure. Pedagogy that assumes later-stage development doesn't
  work.

**What to do:**
- **Model inclusive norms** explicitly. Early norm-setting shapes the
  semester.
- **Make uncertainty safe.** Create spaces where being wrong or confused is
  okay and even expected.
- **Use diverse examples** that don't systematically favor particular
  backgrounds.
- **Be aware of identity-threat cues** in the materials (who is in the
  examples, who's quoted, who's assumed to be the audience).
- **Recognize that building climate takes deliberate work** — it's not a
  side effect of being nice.

**What to look for when critiquing:**
- What messages does the material send about who belongs, who this is for,
  who succeeds?
- Are examples and illustrations drawn from a reasonable range of contexts?
- Is there anything that would feel alienating to a reasonable subset of the
  audience?

## Principle 7: To become self-directed learners, students must learn to monitor and adjust their approaches to learning

Metacognition — the ability to plan, monitor, and regulate one's own learning
— is a skill that has to be taught. Students generally don't develop it
spontaneously, and faculty often overestimate how much students have it.

**What the research says:**
- Metacognitive skill is a strong predictor of learning success, especially
  in college and beyond where self-regulation matters more.
- Students often mismatch effort and outcomes — they study the way they know,
  not the way that works, and they're poor judges of their own understanding
  (e.g., they rate themselves as knowing material they can't actually
  retrieve).
- Metacognition can be taught, but it has to be deliberate — modeled,
  prompted, practiced.

**What to do:**
- **Teach learners to assess the task.** "Before you start, what's the
  assignment actually asking for? What counts as success?"
- **Teach learners to assess themselves.** "What do you already know about
  this? What do you still need to figure out?" And: "Have you actually tested
  your understanding, or just reviewed it?"
- **Model planning, monitoring, and reflecting.** Show your own process
  explicitly. "Here's what I'd do first, here's how I'd check whether it's
  working…"
- **Prompt reflection.** Exam wrappers (after an exam, what strategies worked,
  what didn't, what will you do differently?). Design journals. End-of-unit
  "what did I learn, what's still murky?" prompts.
- **Directly address common misjudgments** — "rereading feels like studying
  but it doesn't work; here's what actually works."

See `practices/effective-techniques.md` for the evidence on which study
techniques actually work versus which students believe work.

**What to look for when critiquing:**
- Are there any metacognitive prompts built in (self-assessment, planning,
  reflection)?
- Does the material teach learners *how to study* it, not just present
  content?
- Are there misconceptions about studying the instructor is leaving
  unchallenged (e.g., that rereading and highlighting are effective)?

## Using these principles together

The seven principles interact. Most pedagogical interventions touch
multiple. Being explicit about objectives (a theme across multiple
principles):

- Helps with prior knowledge (Principle 1) because learners can activate the
  relevant prior knowledge when they know where they're headed.
- Helps with organization (Principle 2) because the objectives expose the
  hierarchy.
- Helps with motivation (Principle 3) because expectations for success are
  clearer.
- Helps with mastery (Principle 4) because component skills are identified.
- Helps with feedback (Principle 5) because there's something specific to give
  feedback against.
- Helps with climate (Principle 6) because fairness is clearer.
- Helps with metacognition (Principle 7) because learners have something to
  monitor against.

When you find a high-leverage place to intervene, it's often because one
change ripples across principles. Prioritize those.

## Summary (useful as a quick checklist)

1. What prior knowledge does this assume, and is it being activated or
   checked?
2. How is the knowledge organization being made visible to the learner?
3. Is motivation supported through relevance, efficacy, and climate?
4. Are component skills being developed, integrated, and practiced in
   recognition?
5. Is practice goal-directed and feedback timely, specific, and actionable?
6. Is the climate supportive and inclusive?
7. Are learners being taught how to monitor and adjust their own learning?
