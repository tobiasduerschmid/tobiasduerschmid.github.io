# Visible Learning: What Actually Moves the Needle

Source: Hattie (2008), *Visible Learning: A Synthesis of Over 800 Meta-Analyses
Relating to Achievement*. Routledge. With updates through subsequent editions.

Hattie's contribution is to take decades of educational research meta-
analyses (now thousands of them) and rank the effect sizes of different
interventions. The headline finding is that *almost everything works*:
roughly 95% of interventions have positive effects on learning. The question
isn't whether something works — it's whether it works *more* than what you'd
get by doing almost anything else.

## The key numbers and what they mean

- **Effect size (Cohen's *d*)** is the standard metric. An effect size of
  0.4 is approximately one year's typical learning progression for a student.
- **0.0** = no effect
- **~0.15** = the effect of simply being in school for a year on average
- **~0.40** = "hinge point" — interventions above this line are meaningfully
  better than the typical effect of schooling
- **Above 0.60** = large effects; the interventions to prioritize
- **Negative effects** exist and are worth flagging (about 5% of interventions)

Before taking any specific effect size too literally: effect sizes from
meta-analyses have substantial uncertainty, depend on study quality and
moderators, and shift over time as new research accumulates. Treat the
*ranking* as more stable than the precise number.

## What's above the hinge point — high-impact practices

These are the practices with *d* > 0.40 — meaningfully above typical year-
of-schooling impact. The list is selective; see the full Visible Learning
for the complete ranking. I've focused on the ones most actionable for
designing and evaluating educational content.

**Feedback** (*d* ≈ 0.70). Hattie describes feedback as the single most
powerful lever — *when it's done well*. Many feedback interventions hover
around zero or negative because the feedback is poor (vague, too late, focused
on the self rather than the task, delivered in a context where the learner
can't act on it). High-quality feedback — task-focused, timely, specific,
actionable — is extraordinarily effective. See `practices/feedback.md`.

**Metacognitive strategies and self-regulation** (*d* ≈ 0.60). Teaching
learners how to plan, monitor, and adjust their own learning.

**Cognitive Task Analysis / Direct Instruction** (*d* ≈ 0.60). Clear
demonstration of what to do, followed by guided practice with scaffolding,
followed by independent practice. The opposite of "figure it out yourself"
pedagogies.

**Reciprocal teaching** (*d* ≈ 0.74). Students take turns leading discussion
using specific cognitive strategies (questioning, clarifying, summarizing,
predicting). A structured form of active learning that scaffolds higher-order
thinking.

**Worked examples** (*d* ≈ 0.57). Fully worked solutions shown before
learners attempt independent practice, especially for novel procedures.
The backbone of the worked-example effect in CLT.

**Spaced versus massed practice** (*d* ≈ 0.60). Distributing study across
time rather than concentrating it. See `practices/effective-techniques.md`.

**Classroom discussion** (*d* ≈ 0.82). When structured — real discussion
requiring argument, not just turn-taking.

**Formative evaluation of teaching** (*d* ≈ 0.90). When teachers gather
evidence about whether their instruction is working and adjust accordingly.
This is what "visible teaching" means — teaching that seeks and responds to
evidence.

**Teacher clarity** (*d* ≈ 0.75). Being clear about what's being taught, why,
how to succeed. Sounds basic; often missing.

**Problem-solving teaching** (*d* ≈ 0.61). Structured instruction in
problem-solving heuristics, not just problems to solve.

**Study skills instruction** (*d* ≈ 0.60). Directly teaching students how
to learn — effective techniques, self-testing, spacing — rather than
assuming they'll figure it out.

**Vocabulary programs** (*d* ≈ 0.62). Direct instruction in discipline-
specific vocabulary before using the vocabulary.

**Meta-cognitive strategies** — a slightly different framing than the first
line, but same idea. Teaching the learner how to learn.

## What's below the hinge point — things often oversold

These are effects that are often advocated for with more confidence than the
evidence supports. They aren't "wrong" — most are still positive — but the
return on investment is lower than common alternatives.

**Inquiry-based learning** (*d* ≈ 0.31). Lower than expected given its
popularity. Inquiry without enough scaffolding produces weak learning.

**Problem-based learning** (*d* ≈ 0.15, with wide variation). Highly
dependent on implementation. Well-done PBL in medical education has large
effects; poorly done has near-zero effects.

**Individualized instruction** (*d* ≈ 0.22). Surprisingly low given how
intuitive it feels. Probably because "individualization" is often
individualization of pace without individualization of *instructional
approach*.

**Web-based learning / online learning** (*d* ≈ 0.18). Lower than traditional
instruction on average, though with enormous variation — some online courses
are excellent and some are terrible. The medium is not the message.

**Matching learning styles** (*d* effectively zero, and the theory is empty).
Teaching to learners' supposed styles (visual, auditory, kinesthetic) has
been thoroughly discredited. Don't design around it; don't recommend it.

**Homework at primary level** (*d* ≈ 0.15). Much smaller than assumed. At
secondary level it's higher (*d* ≈ 0.64), but quality and alignment matter
more than quantity.

**Reducing class size** (*d* ≈ 0.21). Smaller than policy discourse assumes.

**Ability grouping / tracking** (*d* ≈ 0.12). Marginal, and with negative
effects on lower-track students often.

## Negative effects — things to actively avoid

**Retention / being held back a grade** (*d* ≈ -0.13). Among the most
robustly negative effects in the literature. Repeating a grade doesn't help;
it harms.

**Television watching** (*d* ≈ -0.18, though this depends heavily on content).

**Lack of sleep** and **extensive shift work by students** — substantial
negative effects where measured.

## Hattie's overarching synthesis: "Visible teaching, visible learning"

Across all the data, Hattie argues the pattern is that high-impact teaching
has certain features:

1. **Learning is the explicit goal** — not coverage, not activities, not
   engagement. Learning is what success looks like.
2. **Challenge is appropriate** — hard enough to require effort, achievable
   with effort.
3. **Teacher and student both check whether the goal is being met** —
   feedback flowing both directions. The teacher learns what the students are
   learning; the students learn what they're learning.
4. **Deliberate practice toward mastery.** Not just practice, but practice
   aimed at specific skills with feedback.
5. **Feedback is sought and given** — by both teacher and students.
6. **Engagement is active, not passive.**
7. **The teacher is a learner about their own teaching** — monitoring what's
   working, adjusting.
8. **The student becomes their own teacher** — self-regulating, self-
   evaluating, self-monitoring.

The deepest claim in Visible Learning is that **"what teachers do matters"**
— but *what* they do matters more than is often acknowledged. Some teachers
produce much more learning than others, and the differences aren't random;
they track the practices above.

## Using Hattie in critique and design

The practical uses of this body of research:

1. **When someone asks "does X work?"** — Hattie's database probably has an
   answer. If it's in the high-effect zone, advocate for it confidently. If
   it's in the low-effect or negative zone, raise the question honestly.

2. **When making tradeoffs** — use effect sizes as one input. A practice
   with *d* = 0.6 is probably a better investment of effort than one with
   *d* = 0.15, all else equal.

3. **When evaluating a course/material** — look for the high-impact practices
   (feedback quality, worked examples, retrieval/spacing, teacher clarity,
   metacognitive instruction, problem-solving instruction). Their absence is
   often the biggest issue.

4. **When pushing back on interventions that don't have evidence** — matching
   learning styles, individualization of pace, lots of tech for tech's sake.
   The data doesn't support these being high-leverage.

## Caveats

- *Effect size is not destiny.* A practice with average *d* = 0.3 can be
  done brilliantly with a much higher actual effect, or badly with a much
  lower one. Implementation matters.
- *Context matters.* Effect sizes are averages across contexts. The same
  intervention in different populations can produce different effects.
- *Moderators matter.* Often the interesting finding is not "does X work?"
  but "when does X work?"
- *The ranking shifts.* As more research accumulates and as the world
  changes (e.g., technology, student demographics), the specific numbers
  update. Treat the broad ordering as stable; treat specific numbers as
  approximate.
- *Publication bias and methodological concerns.* Meta-analyses aren't
  magic; they inherit the weaknesses of their underlying studies. The
  rough *d* > 0.4 hinge-point framing is pragmatic but not sacred.

## The bottom line

The learning-science research converges on a relatively short list of
practices with reliably large effects: **clear objectives, worked examples,
retrieval and spaced practice, formative feedback, teacher clarity,
metacognitive instruction, classroom discussion, and a teacher who seeks
evidence of student learning and adjusts accordingly**. These show up across
meta-analyses using different methodologies, across subjects, across
grade levels.

When evaluating an educational artifact, asking "does it do these things?"
gets you very far, very fast.
