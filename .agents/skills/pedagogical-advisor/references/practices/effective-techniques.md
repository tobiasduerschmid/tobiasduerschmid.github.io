# Effective Learning Techniques

Source: Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan, & Willingham (2013), "Improving
Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques: Promising Directions
From Cognitive and Educational Psychology," *Psychological Science in the
Public Interest*, 14(1), 4-58. With extensive subsequent research.

Dunlosky et al. is the most influential systematic review of student
study techniques. They rate 10 techniques on their utility for improving
learning, based on breadth of evidence, conditions of effectiveness, and
generalizability across materials, learners, and criterion tasks. The
results are consequential: the techniques students *actually use* tend
to be low-utility. Teaching students to use higher-utility techniques is
among the highest-leverage pedagogical interventions available.

## The ratings

### HIGH utility

**1. Practice testing (retrieval practice)** — taking practice tests,
doing flashcards, answering questions about material without looking.

**2. Distributed practice (spacing)** — spreading study across
multiple sessions over time rather than cramming.

### MODERATE utility

**3. Elaborative interrogation** — asking "why" questions and
generating explanations for facts while studying.

**4. Self-explanation** — explaining aspects of the material to
oneself as one reads or problem-solves.

**5. Interleaved practice** — mixing different types of problems
within a study session rather than blocking them.

### LOW utility

**6. Summarization** — writing summaries of material. Not harmful,
but not strongly effective without specific training.

**7. Highlighting / underlining** — marking parts of text. Essentially
zero effect on most measures. Passive recognition.

**8. Keyword mnemonic** — using keywords and imagery to learn
vocabulary. Useful in narrow cases; doesn't generalize.

**9. Imagery for text** — forming mental images while reading.
Inconsistent effects, mostly specific to certain material types.

**10. Rereading** — reading material multiple times. The default
strategy of many students; essentially zero additional benefit after
the first read-through.

## The two high-utility techniques in depth

### Practice testing (retrieval practice)

**The finding**: requiring learners to retrieve information from memory,
even without feedback, produces dramatically better long-term retention
than additional study of the same material. This is the **testing
effect**.

**What the evidence shows**:
- Retrieval practice produces better retention than re-study on almost
  all tasks tested — factual recall, conceptual questions, transfer
  tasks.
- Effect sizes are often large (*d* > 0.5).
- The effect holds across material types (text, video, procedural),
  age ranges, and educational levels.
- Distributed retrieval (spacing the practice) amplifies the effect.
- Feedback on the retrieval attempt makes it even better, but even
  feedback-free retrieval produces large gains.
- The effect is partly driven by *effortful retrieval*: easy retrieval
  produces smaller gains than retrieval that requires work. Difficulty
  (within reason) is productive here.

**Why it works**:
- The act of retrieving strengthens the memory trace more than
  additional encoding does.
- Retrieval forces the learner to process the structure of what
  they know, not just recognize it.
- Retrieval diagnoses what's actually known vs. what only feels
  familiar. Recognition-based studying (rereading, highlighting)
  produces illusions of knowing.

**Design implications**:
- **Embed retrieval throughout instruction**. Not just at the end of
  a unit, but embedded in readings, lectures, and videos.
- **Low-stakes quizzes** as formative practice. These aren't just
  assessment — they're powerful learning interventions.
- **Self-testing prompts** in textbooks: "Close the book and list
  everything you remember about X." "Explain in your own words why Y."
- **Start sessions with retrieval of prior material** ("warm-ups"
  that call back to last week).
- **Practice testing over rereading**. Teach learners explicitly that
  rereading feels productive but isn't.
- **Recall over recognition** where possible. Short-answer and
  generation prompts are stronger than multiple-choice.

**Design pitfalls**:
- Turning retrieval practice into high-stakes grading undermines the
  effect. Motivation shifts from learning to grade-protection.
- Feedback that only says "right/wrong" without explaining misses part
  of the opportunity. Ideal retrieval practice is followed by
  feedback that addresses the misconception.
- Too-easy retrieval (recognition, cued) produces smaller effects than
  harder retrieval (free recall, generation). But too-hard retrieval
  (complete failure) also underperforms.

### Distributed practice (spacing)

**The finding**: for a given amount of study time, spacing the study
across multiple sessions over time produces dramatically better
long-term retention than massing the same amount in a single session.
This is the **spacing effect**.

**What the evidence shows**:
- The effect is robust across domains, ages, and material types.
- Optimal spacing interval depends on the retention interval: longer
  desired retention → longer optimal spacing.
- A rough heuristic: spaced sessions produce about 2-3x better
  retention than massed sessions with equal total time, when retention
  is measured a week or more later.
- Spacing interacts with retrieval practice — spaced retrieval is more
  effective than either alone.

**Why it works**:
- Between sessions, partial forgetting occurs. The act of re-learning
  strengthens the memory more than continuous exposure does.
- Different sessions encode the material in different contexts, making
  retrieval more context-general.
- Spaced practice forces consolidation between sessions, which
  re-learning doesn't.

**Design implications**:
- **Course-level spacing**: don't cover a topic in week 3 and never
  return to it. Build cumulative assessments; build interleaved review;
  design spiral curricula.
- **Problem sets that include prior material**: weekly problem sets
  that revisit content from previous weeks rather than only the
  current week.
- **Flashcard systems with spaced repetition** (Anki, Leitner box)
  — effective for fact-heavy material.
- **Warm-ups that call back** to earlier material at every session.
- **Final review sessions** structured as retrieval from across the
  course, not re-presentation.

**Design pitfalls**:
- Students strongly prefer massed practice because it feels more
  productive in the moment. It also produces more short-term
  performance. Sustained advocacy is needed to get learners to space.
- Spacing alone without good content and good strategies won't save
  bad instruction.
- Very short intervals (minutes) produce smaller effects than longer
  ones (days/weeks).

## The moderate-utility techniques

### Elaborative interrogation

Prompts like "Why is that true?" or "Why does that make sense?" as the
learner studies. Forces the learner to relate new material to prior
knowledge and generate explanations.

**Design implications**:
- Embed "why" prompts in readings, tutorials, and videos.
- In problem sets, ask for justification not just answer.
- More effective for learners with adequate prior knowledge. If the
  learner genuinely can't answer "why," the technique produces weak
  elaboration or wrong answers.

### Self-explanation

Similar to elaborative interrogation, but broader — explaining aspects
of the material to oneself (steps of a procedure, relationships between
concepts, why something works).

**Design implications**:
- "Explain each step of the worked example to yourself before moving
  on."
- "In your own words, why does X imply Y?"
- Self-explanation after a worked example is especially effective.
- Training the learner to self-explain (not just prompting it) is
  worth the investment for long-term benefit.

### Interleaved practice

Mixing different types of problems within a study session, rather than
blocking them (all problems of type A, then all of type B).

**What the evidence shows**:
- Interleaving feels harder and produces worse performance *during
  practice* than blocking.
- Interleaving produces substantially better performance on subsequent
  tests and on transfer to new material.
- Effect is strongest when the categories are easily confusable —
  interleaving helps learners discriminate.
- Particularly well-established in motor skills and math/problem
  solving.

**Design implications**:
- Problem sets should mix types, not block them.
- Review materials should draw from across the course.
- Teach learners that feeling worse during interleaved practice is a
  sign it's working.
- But: interleave only *after* each type has been learned well enough
  to be recognized. Interleaving from the start can overwhelm.

## The low-utility techniques — and why students love them

Students disproportionately use the low-utility techniques:

- **Rereading** feels productive because it produces fluency — the
  material feels more familiar on re-read. Fluency is a poor proxy
  for learning. Rereading produces *recognition*, not *retrieval
  ability*.
- **Highlighting** produces even weaker gains. It's nearly passive.
  Worse, highlighting often produces the illusion that important
  things are "captured" without engaging with them.
- **Summarization** is closer to effective but requires skill. Poor
  summaries are often rephrasing; good summaries require genuine
  processing.

**The core pedagogical move**: teach learners that feeling like you
know something (familiarity, fluency) is different from being able to
retrieve it. The effective techniques produce more struggle and less
feeling of mastery in the moment, and more actual learning.

## Applying the techniques to instructional design

The principles aren't just for students' self-study. They're design
principles for instructional materials:

**For any content** — embed retrieval practice throughout. Questions in
the margins, end-of-section checks, self-test cards. Don't save all
assessment for summative moments.

**For problem-solving skills** — interleave problem types in practice
sets after each type is established. Don't only block.

**For course-level design** — space content revisits across the term,
not just at the end. Cumulative quizzes. Spiral curricula.

**For study guides** — design them for retrieval practice (questions
with answers hidden), not for re-reading (pure summary).

**For lectures and textbooks** — self-explanation prompts, elaborative
interrogation prompts, embedded recall opportunities.

## Meta-principles

Several broader lessons emerge from Dunlosky's review:

1. **What feels like learning and what is learning are different.**
   Techniques that produce more in-the-moment performance and fluency
   often produce less durable learning. Techniques that feel harder
   often work better.

2. **Desirable difficulties** (Bjork & Bjork, 2011) — productive
   struggle — are a central construct. Spacing is a desirable
   difficulty (harder in the moment, better retention). Retrieval is a
   desirable difficulty. Interleaving is a desirable difficulty.

3. **Teach the learner, not just the material.** A course that
   teaches content well but doesn't teach effective study strategies
   misses a large chunk of the learning potential. Many students
   don't know retrieval practice exists.

4. **Design against the failure modes.** Since students prefer
   low-utility techniques, design materials that make high-utility
   techniques the default path. Built-in quizzes, spaced assignment
   sequences, end-of-section retrieval prompts.

## The Bjorks' "desirable difficulties" framing

Bjork & Bjork's framing is complementary to Dunlosky and worth knowing.
Difficulties that are "desirable" — spacing, retrieval practice,
interleaving, variable practice, generation effects — feel harder and
often produce worse performance in the moment, but substantially better
long-term retention and transfer. Difficulties that are "undesirable"
(confusing wording, poor sequencing, hidden prerequisites) produce
worse performance with no compensating benefit.

The design heuristic: introduce difficulties deliberately where they
correspond to the techniques above, and remove difficulties that don't.

## Critique checklist

When looking at instructional materials or course design:

- Is retrieval practice embedded, or does the material assume rereading
  will do the job?
- Is content spaced across the course, or compartmentalized into units
  that are never revisited?
- Are problems in practice sets interleaved or blocked?
- Are there self-explanation prompts where they'd help?
- Do study guides support retrieval or summary?
- Is any attention given to *how* learners should study the material,
  or only to what they should learn?

## Summary

**Start with retrieval practice and spacing**. These two techniques have
the largest, most robust, most transferable effects on learning. They
should be built into instructional design as defaults, not added as
optional extras.

**Add interleaving and self-explanation** where they fit the material.

**Actively dissuade learners from rereading, highlighting, and
summarizing as primary techniques**. These aren't harmful, but they
produce much less learning than the alternatives.

**Teach the learner to learn**. Metacognitive instruction about what
works is itself a high-impact intervention.
