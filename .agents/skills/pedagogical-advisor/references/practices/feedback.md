# Feedback

Source: Hattie & Timperley (2007), "The Power of Feedback," *Review of
Educational Research*, 77(1), 81-112. Plus the feedback chapters in
Hattie's *Visible Learning* and subsequent work.

Feedback is consistently one of the highest-impact instructional
practices in the education research literature — *when it's done well*.
Many feedback studies produce small or zero effects because the feedback
is poor: too vague, too delayed, too self-focused, or delivered in
contexts where the learner can't act on it. The Hattie & Timperley
framework articulates what makes feedback effective.

## What feedback is for

At its core, feedback is **information that closes the gap between the
learner's current state and the desired state**. This framing has three
implications:

1. There has to be a desired state — a goal, objective, or success
   criterion. Feedback against nothing is nothing.
2. The learner has to be able to identify their current state, either
   from the feedback or on their own.
3. The feedback has to help close the gap — not just describe it.

Bad feedback violates one or more of these. "Good job" violates all
three (no goal, no state, no close-the-gap). "75/100" violates the
third (tells current state vs. max, but not how to improve).

## The Hattie & Timperley model: three questions × four levels

Effective feedback answers three questions for the learner:

- **Feed Up**: "Where am I going?" (What's the goal? What does success
  look like?)
- **Feed Back**: "How am I going?" (How am I doing relative to the goal?)
- **Feed Forward**: "Where to next?" (What's the next step to close the
  gap?)

And feedback operates at four levels, each useful in different contexts:

### Level 1: Feedback about the task (FT)

Information about whether the task was completed correctly. "This
answer is wrong." "You missed problem 3." "The formula here is X, not
Y."

- Useful for correcting surface errors and factual misunderstandings.
- Often the default and most common level.
- Limits: doesn't help the learner do better next time if the issue is
  deeper than this task. Task-level feedback that's purely corrective
  ("here's the right answer") is weaker than task feedback that
  explains why.

### Level 2: Feedback about the process (FP)

Information about the approach or strategy used to do the task. "You
applied the formula correctly but chose the wrong formula for this
problem type." "Your argument is weakened by skipping the objection
step."

- More generalizable than task feedback — addresses the *how*, not
  just the *what*.
- Often more effective for complex learning because it transfers across
  tasks.
- Requires the feedback-giver to actually analyze the process, not
  just judge the product.

### Level 3: Feedback about self-regulation (FSR)

Information about how the learner monitored and controlled their own
work. "Did you check your answer? What would you look for to verify
it?" "When you got stuck, what did you try? What else could you try?"

- Highest-transfer feedback — builds metacognitive capacity.
- Teaches the learner to give themselves feedback going forward.
- Especially powerful for persistent learners who will face novel
  problems.

### Level 4: Feedback about the self as a person (FS)

Praise or criticism of the person. "You're smart." "You're hardworking."
"You're not a math person."

- **Generally the weakest level** and sometimes counterproductive.
- Person-level praise ("you're smart") can trigger fixed-mindset
  responses — the learner starts protecting the label rather than
  engaging with challenges.
- Person-level criticism can be devastating and rarely actionable.
- The occasional positive personal comment isn't harmful, but feedback
  at this level shouldn't dominate.

## The effectiveness pattern

In general, the first three levels can all be effective; the fourth
tends to be weak or harmful.

- **Task-level feedback** is useful but limited.
- **Process-level feedback** is generally the most effective for
  learning.
- **Self-regulation feedback** has the biggest long-term effect because
  it builds the learner's capacity to continue without you.
- **Self-level feedback** should be used sparingly.

The pattern within task-level feedback is also informative: *corrective*
task feedback (here's the right answer with brief explanation) is more
effective than pure *evaluative* task feedback (just right/wrong).

## What makes feedback effective in practice

Across the research, high-impact feedback shares several features:

### Specific

Generic feedback ("good job", "needs work") does little. Specific
feedback ("your thesis statement previews the argument clearly",
"your argument doesn't address the strongest counterargument") gives
the learner something to act on.

### Timely

Feedback is most useful when the learner can still act on it. Feedback
on a draft that will be revised is useful; feedback on a final submission
with no revision opportunity is mostly historical. Timing also affects
motivation — too-delayed feedback loses emotional connection to the
work.

But: *too* immediate can be worse for retention in some cases. In
retrieval practice, delayed feedback after attempted recall often
produces better learning than immediate feedback. The tradeoff is
context-dependent.

### Actionable

The feedback makes clear what the learner should do differently next
time. "You missed the objection step in paragraph 3" is better than
"your argument is incomplete." "Consider applying the rate law from
Chapter 4 here" is better than "wrong approach."

### Goal-referenced

Feedback refers to the success criteria or learning objectives, not
just the instructor's idiosyncratic preferences. This requires success
criteria that are explicit — which is why rubrics and learning
objectives are load-bearing.

### Calibrated to the learner's level

- For struggling learners, process-level feedback is often overwhelming
  — they need task-level clarity first. Too-high-level feedback to a
  struggling learner can feel inscrutable.
- For competent learners, task-level feedback is often too granular —
  they need process and self-regulation feedback to keep growing.
- High-performing learners often get less feedback than they need
  because they're "already doing fine." This caps their growth.

### Low-threat

Feedback delivered in a context where the learner feels safe to be
wrong produces more learning than feedback in a judgmental context.
The learner who's defending their self-worth can't engage with the
content of the feedback.

## Feedback that often fails

- **Vague praise or criticism.** "Well done." "Needs improvement."
  Learner can't extract anything actionable.
- **Grade-only feedback.** A number or letter without specifics.
  Research shows learners who get only a grade learn substantially less
  than those who get only comments — and adding a grade to comments
  often *decreases* engagement with the comments.
- **Feedback the learner can't act on.** Final-exam feedback after
  the course ends. Feedback on a single-submission assignment with no
  revision mechanism.
- **Feedback delivered with a tone that triggers defensiveness.**
  Harsh, sarcastic, or implicitly shaming feedback often gets rejected
  by the learner regardless of its accuracy.
- **Feedback that overwhelms.** A paper returned with every line marked
  is unmanageable; the learner can't prioritize and often disengages.
- **Feedback that contradicts other signals.** "Great paper, C+" is
  incoherent and teaches learners that the feedback is unreliable.

## The SBI (Situation-Behavior-Impact) structure

A practical structure for delivering constructive feedback:

- **Situation**: where/when was this observed. "In paragraph 3…"
- **Behavior**: what specifically was done. "…you jumped from premise
  to conclusion without addressing the objection…"
- **Impact**: what effect this has. "…which weakens your argument
  because a skeptical reader would land on that objection."
- (Optional) **Recommendation/Reflection**: what to try next. "Try
  adding a sentence that concedes and responds to the objection."

This structure keeps feedback specific, observable, and impact-focused.
It's widely used in professional contexts and works for educational
feedback too.

## Peer feedback

Peer feedback has real benefits — the learner giving feedback often
learns more than the learner receiving it — but also clear limits:

- **Quality**: peer feedback is variable. Training peers in what good
  feedback looks like is essential.
- **Specificity**: without structure (rubric, prompts), peer feedback
  tends toward vague praise or personal preference.
- **Trust**: peer feedback is often discounted by the receiver without
  an instructor's imprimatur.
- **Design move**: use peer feedback for drafts and formative moments;
  provide structured prompts; follow up with instructor feedback.

## Feedback across the course — systemic view

Most feedback design focuses on individual feedback moments. But
systemic design matters too:

- **Feedback frequency**: more frequent, lower-stakes is generally
  better than infrequent, high-stakes.
- **Feedback loops**: can the learner see their improvement? Do later
  assignments give feedback that references earlier feedback?
- **Feedback on progress, not just performance**: Is the learner
  getting better? Where are they growing?
- **Self-feedback scaffolds**: Are learners being taught to generate
  their own feedback through checklists, rubrics, self-assessment
  prompts?

## Critique checklist

When looking at an instructional artifact for its feedback
architecture:

- What goals or success criteria are stated? Are they specific enough
  to support feedback?
- What mechanisms exist for the learner to get feedback? At what
  intervals?
- Is the feedback task-, process-, or self-regulation-level? Or
  person-level?
- Is the feedback specific enough to be actionable?
- Can the learner use the feedback — is there an opportunity to apply
  it?
- Does the feedback refer to goals/rubrics?
- Is there attention to the climate in which feedback is given?

When critiquing an artifact where you're providing feedback yourself
(as the pedagogical advisor), these principles also apply to you. Your
feedback should:

- Be specific (cite locations, quote)
- Be process-level as well as task-level (why something matters, not
  just that it's missing)
- Be actionable (what to change)
- Acknowledge strengths as well as weaknesses
- Be calibrated to what the creator can do (not overwhelming)
- Be honest but not harsh

## Summary

**Feedback is among the highest-leverage interventions — if done well.**

The Hattie & Timperley model:
- Feed-up: where are we going?
- Feed-back: how are we doing?
- Feed-forward: what's next?

Across four levels:
- Task (useful but limited)
- Process (often most effective)
- Self-regulation (highest transfer)
- Self (weak, sometimes harmful)

**Make it specific, timely, actionable, goal-referenced, and
low-threat**, and feedback becomes one of the most powerful tools in
educational design. **Leave any of those out**, and feedback becomes
one of the most common null-result interventions.
